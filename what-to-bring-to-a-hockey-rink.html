Parent Guide

What to Bring to a Hockey Rink

The complete packing guide for hockey parents. Never show up missing something again.

🏒 All Levels · 5 min read · Built by hockey families
01

Your Player's Gear Bag

This is the stuff that goes in the bag every single time. Teach your player to check this list themselves — the sooner they own it, the fewer 6am panics you'll have.

Skates
Check the blades before you leave. Dull skates ruin practices. When in doubt, sharpen.
Required
Helmet with Cage or Shield
USA Hockey requires a cage or shield for all youth players. No exceptions at any level.
Required
Neck Guard
Required by USA Hockey. A non-negotiable even if your kid protests.
Required
Shoulder Pads
Should fit snugly — not so loose they shift during play.
Required
Elbow Pads
Cover the elbow cap and forearm. Check fit every season — kids grow fast.
Required
Gloves
Fingers should reach the end but not feel cramped. Wrist protection matters at higher levels.
Required
Shin Guards
Should cover from just above the ankle to just below the knee. Tuck into socks properly.
Required
Hockey Pants (Breezers)
Cover the hips, thighs, and tailbone. Don't let your kid skate without these.
Required
Jock or Jill
Always buy new. Always. Non-negotiable for boys and girls both.
Required
Hockey Socks + Sock Tape
Socks go over shin guards. Tape at the top to keep them in place. Keep a roll in the bag.
Required
Jersey and Practice Shorts or Pants
Team jersey for games. Practice jersey or old shirt for practices. Check with your coach.
Required
Hockey Stick
Bring a backup if you have one. Sticks break. Games don't pause for broken sticks.
Required
Mouthguard
Required at most levels. Boil-and-bite works fine. Keep a spare in the bag.
Required
Water Bottle
Labeled with your player's name. Rinks are dry — they need to hydrate before, during, and after.
Required
Quick Tip

Ready to hand off bag duty? Check out the Player's Packing Guide — written for your kid, not you.

02

Smart Extras to Keep in the Bag

These aren't required, but after a few seasons you'll wonder how you survived without them.

Extra Skate Laces
Laces break at the worst times. Keep one spare pair per skate size in the bag. $3 insurance.
Highly Recommend
Sock Tape + Hockey Tape
Black or white tape for the stick blade. Sock tape for keeping shin guard socks up. Both live in the bag permanently.
Highly Recommend
Skate Guards
Protect blades walking to and from the ice. Not for storing skates — take them off in the locker room.
Highly Recommend
Small Towel
For wiping down blades after skating. Dry blades = no rust. Takes 10 seconds. Skates last much longer.
Highly Recommend
Snack for After
Players burn real energy out there. A banana, granola bar, or peanut butter sandwich goes a long way after practice. Especially for early morning skates.
Highly Recommend
Dry Bag or Mesh Bag for Wet Gear
Keep wet gear from soaking everything else in the bag on the way home. A $5 mesh laundry bag works fine.
Highly Recommend
Sharpie (Permanent Marker)
Label everything. Sticks, helmets, gloves, water bottles. Gear goes missing in locker rooms. If it has a name on it, it usually comes back.
Nice to Have
Backup Mouthguard
One lost mouthguard has cancelled more practices than you'd think. Keep a spare boil-and-bite in the bag.
Nice to Have
03

What Parents Should Wear

First-time rink parents always underestimate this. Hockey rinks are cold — usually between 50–60°F at ice level, cooler if you're rinkside. Here's what works.

Wear This
  • + Base layer or thermal undershirt
  • + Fleece or light jacket
  • + Warm socks — thick ones
  • + Closed-toe shoes you can stand in for an hour
  • + Light gloves for early morning practices
  • + Hat or beanie if you run cold
Skip This
  • − Flip flops or sandals
  • − Shorts (you will regret it)
  • − That one light cardigan that "should be fine"
  • − High heels or dress shoes near the ice
From Experience

Keep a dedicated "rink bag" in your car — a fleece, a pair of thick socks, hand warmers, and a blanket for tournament bleachers. You'll be glad you have it. So will every hockey parent you're sitting next to.

04

What Parents Should Bring

Beyond dressing warm, here's what experienced hockey parents keep in their rink bag or car.

Your Own Water or Coffee
Rink concessions are hit or miss and often expensive. A travel mug of coffee for a 6am practice is non-negotiable after your first season.
Highly Recommend
Chair or Bleacher Cushion
Rink bleachers are hard metal or cold plastic. A small stadium cushion makes a two-hour tournament stretch survivable.
Highly Recommend
Cash for the Snack Bar
Many rink snack bars still don't take cards. A $20 in your pocket has saved many a hungry sibling situation.
Highly Recommend
Something for Siblings
A book, small activity bag, or coloring supplies. An hour of watching your sibling skate is a long time when you're 6 years old.
Highly Recommend
Phone Charger or Portable Battery
You will be at rinks more than you think. Outlets are rare. A portable battery pack earns its weight.
Nice to Have
Small First Aid Kit
Band-aids, ibuprofen, and a cold pack. For the kid who takes a stick to the shin in practice and needs something before you get home.
Nice to Have
05

Tournament Day Extras

Regular practice and games are one thing. A multi-day tournament is a different animal. Here's what separates the families who are comfortable from the ones who are miserable by day two.

Extra Socks and Underclothes
Gear gets wet. Multiple games in a day means wet gear going back on. Bring extra hockey socks, regular socks, underwear, and a fresh shirt. Dry everything is a gift.
Tournament Must
Healthy Snacks — More Than You Think
Tournament food is usually hot dogs and nachos. Bring bananas, trail mix, PB&J, and real water. Pack enough for between games, after the last game, and before bed — tournament kids are hungry all day and into the night.
Tournament Must
Team Schedule Printed or Saved
Don't rely on wifi at the rink. Download or print the schedule before you leave the hotel. Tournament brackets change and you need to know immediately.
Tournament Must
Backup Stick Tape
Tape unwraps mid-tournament constantly. A fresh roll is 10 minutes of pre-game peace.
Recommended
Team Parent Contact Info
Know who's managing communication for the team. A group text or TeamSnap connection before you leave home saves a lot of confusion at a busy tournament facility.
Recommended
The Thing Nobody Tells You

Multi-day tournaments mean full days at the rink. Pack like you're going to be there all day, because you are. Bring real food, dress in layers, and assume the wifi won't work. The families who show up prepared enjoy it. The ones who don't are miserable by noon on day one.

06

The Things You'll Forget Once

Every hockey parent has a story. Here are the most common things that get left behind — so you don't have to learn the hard way.

The Skates
Yes, this happens. Usually because they were drying in the garage. Check the bag the night before, not in the parking lot.
Classic Mistake
The Helmet
Left on the shelf in the garage after airing out. Add it to the bag the night before every time.
Classic Mistake
The Stick
Leaning against the wall by the front door. Make a rule: if it's not in the bag, it doesn't exist.
Classic Mistake
The Water Bottle
Still in the dishwasher. Wet or not, it goes in the bag the night before.
Classic Mistake
The Mouth Guard
On the bathroom counter, still in the plastic case. Keep a spare in the bag permanently.
Classic Mistake
The Real Fix

Pack the bag the night before. Every time. No exceptions. It takes five minutes when you're not rushing and saves 20 minutes of chaos on a 5:45am game day. This is the single habit that separates the calm hockey families from the stressed ones.
