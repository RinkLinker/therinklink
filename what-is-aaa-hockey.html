Hockey Parent Guide

What Is AAA Hockey —
And Is Your Athlete Ready?

The straight-talk guide every hockey family needs before chasing the top level of youth hockey.

🏒 For Hockey Parents · ⏱ 8 min read · 📍 Colorado & Beyond
01

First — What Does AAA Even Mean?

Youth hockey is organized into levels based on skill and competition. Most people have heard the terms — house, travel, AA, AAA — but few new hockey parents actually know what separates them.

Here's the quick breakdown:

House
Recreational. Local league, mostly for fun and learning. No tryouts, everyone plays.
A / AA
Competitive travel hockey. Tryouts required. Practices multiple times a week. Tournaments on weekends.
AAA
Tier 1 — the highest level of youth hockey. Elite travel, intense schedules, major time and financial commitment.
Tier 1 / Tier 2
USA Hockey's official designations. AAA = Tier 1. AA = Tier 2. Both are competitive travel.
💡 The Simple Version

AAA is the top of the youth hockey pyramid. It's where the best players in a region compete against each other, with the goal of developing athletes who could play Junior, college, or beyond.

02

What AAA Actually Looks Like

Before you pursue AAA, you need to understand what you're signing up for — because it's a major lifestyle commitment, not just a hockey commitment.

  • 🧊
    4–6 ice sessions per week. On-ice practice, off-ice training, and games. Your weeknights belong to hockey.
  • ✈️
    Regional and national travel. Tournaments often mean flights, hotels, and missed school. A 30–40 game season is normal, plus playoffs.
  • 💪
    Year-round commitment. AAA programs don't really have an "off-season." Summer skating, dryland training, and development camps fill the gaps.
  • 👨‍👩‍👧
    Full family buy-in. Siblings, spouses, and schedules all get rearranged. This affects the whole household, not just the player.
  • 🏫
    School requires planning. Early morning skates, missed Fridays for travel, and fatigue from games all need active management with teachers and administration.
⚠️ Honest Truth

Many families underestimate the time commitment. Talk to current AAA families — not the brochure version, but real parents in the parking lot — before you commit.

03

What Does AAA Cost?

This is the question everyone wants to ask and nobody wants to answer honestly. Here's the reality for a typical AAA season in Colorado:

Expense Typical Range Notes
Program fees (dues) $8,000 – $14,000 Varies widely by organization and age level
Tournament fees $2,000 – $5,000 Travel, entry fees, hotel; some included in dues
Equipment $1,000 – $2,500 Full gear refresh every 1–2 years at this level
Private coaching $1,500 – $4,000 Optional but common — skating coaches, skills coaches
Off-ice training $500 – $2,000 Gym, sports performance, dryland programs
Stick costs $500 – $1,500 Top-level sticks break. Budget accordingly.
Total annual estimate $13,000 – $29,000+ Per player, per year
💡 Ask Before You Commit

Get a full itemized breakdown from any AAA program before signing. Ask specifically what is and isn't included in dues — tournament travel, equipment, and private coaching can add up fast on top of base fees.

04

Is Your Athlete Actually Ready?

Skill matters — but it's not the only thing. AAA programs are looking for athletes who are ready physically, mentally, and emotionally. Here's an honest self-assessment:

  • ✅
    Skating is elite for their age group. AAA players skate faster, harder edges, better backward skating than their peers. If your kid is one of the top skaters at AA tryouts, that's a good sign.
  • ✅
    Hockey IQ stands out. Reading the ice, positioning, seeing plays develop — coachable, smart hockey sense. This matters as much as raw speed.
  • ✅
    They love the game — not you. The most important question: does your child want this, or do they want to make you happy? AAA demands come from inside the player, not from the parents.
  • ✅
    They can handle failure. Getting cut from a line, losing a tournament, a bad game — AAA players face adversity constantly. Resilience is a requirement.
  • ✅
    Physically mature enough. Early specialization at very young ages (8U, 10U) is controversial. Many experts recommend multi-sport play until 12–14 before full AAA commitment.
  • ⚠️
    Red flag: parent is more excited than the kid. It happens all the time. Be honest with yourself.
05

How to Evaluate a AAA Program

Not all AAA programs are created equal. Some are excellent development environments. Others are expensive and poorly run. Here's what to look for:

  • 🔍
    Talk to current families — not the organization. Reach out to parents on the current roster. Ask about communication, ice time quality, coaching style, and whether they'd do it again.
  • 🔍
    Evaluate the coaching staff. What's their playing background? Do they have USA Hockey certifications? How do they handle player development vs. just winning?
  • 🔍
    Ask about ice time quantity and quality. How many hours of ice per week? Where do they practice? Is it quality rink time or early morning terrible ice?
  • 🔍
    Look at player advancement. Where do alumni go? Are players moving on to Junior hockey and college programs, or does the pipeline stop at 18U?
  • 🔍
    Understand their culture. Is it development-first or win-at-all-costs? Both exist. Know which one you're buying into.
⚠️ Watch Out For

Programs that pressure families at tryouts to commit on the spot. A reputable AAA organization gives you time to ask questions and think it over. High-pressure sales tactics at tryout day are a red flag.

06

AAA vs. AA — The Real Difference

For many families, AA is the right answer. Here's the honest comparison most people don't want to have:

  • 🏒
    AA is still elite hockey. Strong AA programs produce college players and Junior hockey players. Don't let anyone tell you AA is "just for kids who can't make AAA."
  • ⏰
    AA gives you some of your life back. Fewer practices per week, less travel, more family time. For a 10-year-old, that matters.
  • 💰
    AA costs significantly less. Typically $3,000–$7,000 per year all-in. That gap compounds fast over a 6-year youth hockey career.
  • 📈
    Late developers thrive at AA. Many players who bloom at 14–16 started at AA and moved up. The path to high-level hockey isn't always through AAA at age 9.
💡 The Bottom Line

If your kid loves hockey and is developing well, AA is a great place to be. Don't let other hockey parents pressure you into a decision that doesn't fit your family. The best hockey path is the one your athlete is happy on.
