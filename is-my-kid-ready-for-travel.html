Is My Kid Ready for Travel?

Skill is only part of the question. Here's how to actually tell if your kid, and your family, are ready for the jump.

🏒 Travel Teams · 7 min read · Built by hockey families
01

Skill Is the Easy Part to Judge

Most parents ask the wrong question first. They ask whether their kid is good enough, when the more useful question is whether their kid actually wants this, badly enough to handle the parts that have nothing to do with skill at all.

Kids who love the game for its own sake, not just for winning or for a coach's praise, tend to hold up better once travel gets hard. They keep showing up for practice even after a rough game. They stay engaged on the bench instead of checking out. That kind of engagement is a real signal, and it's one you can watch for at your current level before a single tryout happens.

Ability still matters, and coaches will absolutely evaluate it. But ability without the desire to grind through a harder season tends to burn out fast, while desire without much polish yet has real room to grow. If you're only weighing one side of that, you're missing half the picture.

02

What This Actually Looks Like at Home

Abstract enthusiasm is hard to measure. Concrete behavior isn't. A kid who's genuinely pulling toward more hockey usually shows it in small, unprompted ways: shooting pucks in the driveway without being asked, requesting extra open skate time, watching game highlights or full NHL games on their own, asking questions about players or positions that go beyond what you'd expect from someone just going through the motions.

None of that guarantees travel is the right move this year. Kids are inconsistent, and a kid who's obsessed with hockey in July can feel differently by October once school and other activities are back in the mix. Treat these as signals worth noticing, not a checklist that locks in a decision.

Worth a Closer Look
  • ✓ Asks to go to the rink on off days
  • ✓ Bounces back from a bad game by the next practice
  • ✓ Follows instruction from coaches without a fight
  • ✓ Talks about teammates, not just their own stats
Worth Waiting On
  • · Dreads practice more often than not
  • · Only you are pushing for the move up
  • · Struggles to recover emotionally from mistakes
  • · Already stretched thin by school or other sports
03

What Coaches Are Actually Watching For

At tryouts, coaches aren't just ranking who's fastest. They're looking at a foundation: skating, stickhandling, and shooting that's solid enough to build on, plus whether a kid can take instruction and function inside a team structure rather than freelancing the whole game. A kid who's still shaky on edges or crossovers isn't necessarily a lost cause, but that gap tends to show up fast once the pace picks up at travel level.

Worth Asking Your Current Coach

Most house league and mite-level coaches will tell you honestly, if you ask directly, whether your kid looks ready for a travel evaluation. They've watched your kid against the same group of peers all season and usually have a clearer read than you do on how that compares to travel-level expectations.

04

Age Matters Less Than People Assume

There's no single age where a kid becomes ready for travel. Some associations start travel programs as young as seven or eight, others wait until the mite-to-squirt transition around age nine, when full-ice games and more competitive structure typically kick in. Readiness is closer to a range than a specific birthday, and a kid who isn't ready at eight can be more than ready at ten.

What the Research Actually Says

USA Hockey and sports medicine researchers generally recommend that young players stay in multiple sports rather than specializing in hockey year-round this early. Kids who play a different sport each season tend to build more varied movement patterns and show lower rates of overuse injury than kids locked into one sport full time.

That's a real argument for not rushing the move to a heavier travel schedule just because a spot is available this year. Waiting a season, or keeping a second sport in the mix once travel starts, isn't holding your kid back. For a lot of families it's what makes the whole thing sustainable.

Source: USA Hockey's long-term athlete development guidance, consistent with broader sports medicine research on youth specialization

05

The Family Side of the Decision

Even a kid who's completely ready doesn't make travel work on their own. Someone has to drive to practice three or four times a week, get to tournaments some weekends, and absorb a cost that runs well past a house league registration fee. Ask honestly whether that's realistic for your household this season, not just whether it would be nice in theory.

It also helps to remember this isn't a permanent decision. Kids move between house and travel, and between travel levels, more often than people expect. Saying not this year isn't closing a door.
