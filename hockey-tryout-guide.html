Hockey Parent & Player Guide

The Hockey Tryout Guide

What to expect, how to prepare, what evaluators look for — and how to handle whatever the result is.

🏒 Players & Parents · ⏱ 10 min read · 📍 All Levels
01

What to Expect at Tryouts

Tryouts vary by organization and age level, but most follow a similar structure. Knowing what's coming takes away a lot of the anxiety for both players and parents.

  • 📋
    Registration and numbers. Players are assigned a number worn on their jersey or helmet so evaluators can track individuals without knowing names. Keep the number visible at all times.
  • 🧊
    Multiple ice sessions. Most programs run 2–4 tryout sessions over a week or two. No one gets cut after the first skate — evaluators want to see consistency over multiple days.
  • ⚡
    Skating drills first. Expect edge work, crossovers, transitions, and speed drills. Skating is the #1 thing evaluators watch. It sets the tone for everything else.
  • 🏒
    Puck skills and shooting. Stickhandling courses, passing drills, shooting accuracy. Some programs do 1-on-1 or small-area games.
  • 🎮
    Scrimmages. The scrimmage is where hockey IQ gets evaluated. Decision-making, positioning, compete level — this is where players can really stand out.
💡 For Parents

You will likely watch from the stands. Don't coach from the glass — evaluators notice parents doing this and it reflects poorly. Let your player play their game.

02

How to Prepare Physically

You can't cram for a tryout the week before — but there are things both players and families can do in the weeks leading up to it.

🏒 For the Player

  • Get on ice as much as possible in the 4–6 weeks prior — stick & puck, public skate, whatever you can access
  • Work on your weakest skating edge — evaluators notice the gaps
  • Get enough sleep the week of tryouts — tired legs show immediately on ice
  • Eat a solid meal 2–3 hours before, not right before you skate
  • Arrive early — rushing to get dressed adds stress before you even step on the ice
  • Bring water and a snack for between sessions

👨‍👩‍👧 For the Parent

  • Have all gear clean, dry, and ready the night before — no scrambling morning of
  • Know where you're going and plan for parking — rinks can get chaotic on tryout days
  • Keep conversations about the tryout positive and low-pressure at home
  • Don't compare your player to others on the way to the rink
  • Have a low-key plan for after — win or lose, the drive home sets the tone
03

Managing Nerves & Mental Prep

Nerves are normal. A little adrenaline actually helps. But anxiety that spirals can tank a tryout for a talented player. Here's how to keep it useful.

  • 🧠
    Reframe it: excitement, not fear. The physical feeling of nervousness and excitement is identical. Teach your player to tell themselves "I'm excited" instead of "I'm nervous." It actually works.
  • 🎯
    Focus on effort, not outcome. Before the skate, set a simple goal like "skate hard on every drill" or "communicate on the ice." Something controllable — not "make the team."
  • 🌬️
    Breathe before stepping on the ice. Three slow, deep breaths before you go through the gate. Simple and surprisingly effective.
  • 🎵
    Music in the locker room helps. Many players have a pre-game playlist. Encourage your player to have headphones and a playlist they love for the dressing room.
  • 💬
    Parents: watch what you say in the car. "Just do your best" is fine. "This is your big chance" is not. Kids internalize pressure from parents more than we realize.
✅ The Best Mental Prep

The players who perform best at tryouts are usually the ones who focus on playing their game — not trying to impress anyone. Remind your player: just play hockey. Do what you do.

04

What Evaluators Are Looking For

Every evaluator is different, but most youth hockey programs assess players across the same core categories. Here's what's actually being scored:

01

Skating — The #1 Factor

Speed, edges, crossovers, backward skating, transitions. Skating separates levels more than any other skill. A player who skates exceptionally well will always get a long look, even if other skills need development.

02

Hockey Sense / IQ

Does the player see the ice? Do they know where to be before the puck gets there? Do they make smart decisions under pressure? This is evaluated heavily in scrimmages and can compensate for physical limitations.

03

Compete Level & Battle

Does the player compete hard on every drill — even the boring ones? Do they win puck battles? Effort is visible on every shift and evaluators weight it heavily, especially at younger ages.

04

Puck Skills

Stickhandling control, passing accuracy, shooting with intention. A player doesn't need to be flashy — clean, reliable puck handling scores well.

05

Coachability

Does the player listen and adjust when a coach gives instruction during the tryout? This matters more than most parents expect. Evaluators are also building a team culture — they want coachable kids.

06

Consistency Across Sessions

One great session doesn't make a roster. Evaluators want to see who shows up the same way every day. Consistency signals reliability over a full season.

⚠️ What Evaluators Also Notice

Players who pout after mistakes, argue with coaches, or coast on drills. These are red flags regardless of skill level. Character is part of the evaluation — always.

05

If Your Player Gets Cut

Getting cut is one of the hardest experiences in youth sports — for the player and for the parent. How you handle it matters enormously, both for your child's development and for their relationship with the game.

🏒 For the Player

  • Getting cut is not a verdict on you as a person — it's one evaluation on one day
  • Every great hockey player has been cut at some point
  • Ask the coach for feedback — then actually use it
  • Give yourself a day to be disappointed. Then make a plan.
  • The players who come back stronger after a cut are the ones who make teams the next year

👨‍👩‍👧 For the Parent

  • Don't say anything negative about the coaches, the organization, or the process in front of your kid
  • Let them feel the disappointment — don't rush to fix it
  • Ask "what do you want to do?" before making any decisions about next steps
  • Wait 48 hours before reaching out to the organization for feedback
  • A different team or level might be a better fit — and that's genuinely okay
💡 The Drive Home Rule

Many hockey families live by a simple rule: nothing about the tryout on the drive home. Just "I love watching you play." Let them decompress. The conversation can happen later — when emotions have settled for everyone.

✅ What to Do Next

If your player is serious about hockey, a cut is information — not a stop sign. Request feedback from the evaluating coach, identify the specific gaps, and build a development plan for the next 6–12 months. Players who work on what they were told after a cut almost always improve their result the next year.
