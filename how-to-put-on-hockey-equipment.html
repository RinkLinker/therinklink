How to Put On Hockey Equipment

The right order, the right fit, and the tips every new hockey family needs before first skate.

🏒 For New Hockey Families · ⏱ 5 min read · 🧊 Works for All Ages

Before You Start — The Golden Rules

Hockey equipment has to go on in a specific order — the bottom layers get covered by the top layers, and straps from one piece thread through or over the next. Put it on wrong and something won't fit right, or worse, won't protect properly.

💡 Pro Parent Tip

Give yourself 20–30 minutes the first few times. Experienced families can dress a kid in 10 minutes. You'll get there. Lay everything out on a bench in order before you start — it makes a huge difference.

Step-by-Step — Full Dressing Guide

1
Base Layer (Compression Shirt & Shorts)
Start with a moisture-wicking base layer — compression shorts and a fitted long-sleeve shirt. Cotton is not your friend. It holds sweat and gets cold fast. Synthetic or wool base layers keep players dry and warm.
💡 Many players skip a base layer at first — don't. It makes a big comfort difference, especially in cold rinks.
2
Jock (Boys) or Jill (Girls)
This goes on over the base layer shorts. Boys wear a jock cup (either a traditional jock strap or compression shorts with a built-in cup). Girls wear a jill, which is a pelvic protector with a different fit. This piece is non-negotiable — it's required by USA Hockey for all youth players.
💡 Many players use compression shorts with a built-in cup pocket — cleaner and more comfortable than a traditional jock strap for younger players.
3
Shin Guards
Slide the shin guards up the front of the leg, with the knee cap centered on the kneecap. The guard should cover from just above the ankle to just above the knee. The straps go around the back of the calf — usually two straps, velcro or elastic. They should feel snug, not tight.
💡 Proper fit check: have your player bend their knee. The guard should stay in place and not slide down or bind.
4
Hockey Socks
These are long, thin socks that go over the shin guards and are held up by the hockey pants above. Pull them up over the guards so the sock covers the shin pad completely. They attach to the hockey pants via velcro tabs or a garter belt inside the pants.
💡 Many newer hockey pants have built-in velcro inside the bottom hem — the socks attach right to the pants. If not, a garter belt goes around the waist before the pants.
5
Hockey Pants (Breezers)
Step into the pants and pull them up to the waist. They should sit at the natural waist, not the hips. Fasten the velcro or belt closure at the front. The bottom of the pants should overlap the top of the shin guards — no gap between them.
💡 The sock tabs inside the pants attach to the hockey socks now. Pull them down and press the velcro together to hold the socks up.
6
Skates
Put skates on now, before the upper body equipment — it's much easier to lace up without shoulder pads in the way. Lace them snugly from the toe up, getting progressively tighter toward the top. The ankle should be snug and supported. The toe should have a small amount of wiggle room.
💡 New skates need to be baked (heat-molded) for the best fit. Most pro shops offer this. Don't skip it for new players — it prevents blisters and dramatically improves comfort.
7
Shoulder Pads
Slide on over the head like a vest. The shoulder caps should sit squarely on the shoulders — not sliding forward or back. The chest plate protects the sternum. The bottom of the shoulder pads should slightly overlap the top of the hockey pants — no gap exposing the ribs.
💡 If they're loose, use the straps underneath to tighten around the chest and back. A wobbly shoulder pad is a poorly fitting one.
8
Elbow Pads
Slide up the arm with the hard cap centered directly on the elbow. Fasten the velcro straps — usually two, one above and one below the elbow. They should feel secure but not cut off circulation. The bottom of the elbow pad should overlap the top of the glove when the arm is bent.
💡 Have your player bend their elbow fully — the pad should stay centered on the elbow, not slide up or down.
9
Jersey
Pull the jersey on over everything. In games, jerseys are tucked into the pants in the back and hang loose in the front — or fully tucked depending on your team's preference. For practice, just pull it on over everything. The jersey should be loose enough to move freely over all the padding.
10
Neck Guard
The neck guard wraps around the neck and fastens at the back or front. It protects against skate blade contact — required for all youth players in USA Hockey. It should fit snugly around the neck without being tight enough to restrict breathing or movement.
💡 Some helmets have an integrated neck guard as a bib — check if your helmet includes one before buying separately.
11
Helmet & Cage/Visor
Place the helmet on the head and fasten the chin cup firmly. The helmet should not rock forward, backward, or side to side when you push on it. Adjust the fit dial or pads inside until it's snug. Attach the cage (required for youth players) — it should click into both sides and hang straight in front of the face.
💡 The two-finger test: you should be able to fit two fingers between the cage and the chin. If the cage touches the nose or chin, it's too low.
12
Gloves — Last On, First Off
Gloves go on last because you need your hands free for everything else. Slide them on and flex the fingers — the cuff of the glove should overlap the elbow pad when the arm is extended. No gap. Gloves come off first when undressing because you need your hands to remove everything else.
💡 Never leave the locker room without gloves on. They go on at the bench, not on the ice.

Common New Parent Mistakes

⚠️ Shin Guards Under the Socks

The shin guards go on first, then the hockey socks go over them. Not the other way around.

⚠️ Skates Too Loose

New players often complain skates feel tight. That's usually correct — they should be snug. Too loose means ankle folds, which causes falls and frustration. When in doubt, have a pro shop check the fit.

⚠️ Gap Between Pants and Shin Guards

If you can see bare skin or base layer between the pants and the shin guard, the pants are too high or the shin guards are too short. Everything should overlap.

⚠️ Forgetting the Neck Guard

It's easy to forget — it goes on just before the helmet. USA Hockey requires it for all youth players. No neck guard, no ice.

💡 The Fastest Way to Learn

Watch the experienced hockey parents in your locker room. They'll know every shortcut. Most hockey families are happy to help first-timers — just ask.

After the Skate — Drying and Care

Equipment that doesn't dry properly gets funky fast. A few habits will keep gear lasting longer and smelling better:

Air it out immediately. Don't leave gear in a bag after practice. Hang it up — most families use a drying rack or hang it over a laundry basket in the garage.

Dry skates after every use. Wipe the blades dry before putting skate guards on. Wet blades rust. Skate guards go on for transport only — never store skates in guards long-term.

Wash what you can. Base layers, socks, and jerseys go in the wash after every use. Chest pads and pants can be washed periodically. Helmets can be wiped down inside with a damp cloth.

Get a gear bag with ventilation. Hockey bags with mesh panels or separate wet pockets make a big difference in how fast gear dries on the way home.
