One Year In: Taking Stock

By one year in, you know things the first few months couldn't show you. Real questions to ask your kid, and how to carry the answers into what comes next.

🏒 Travel Teams · 7 min read · Built by hockey families
01

What a Full Season Actually Tells You

By the one-year mark, you know things a first few months couldn't have shown you. You've seen how your kid handles the schedule, how the coach communicates, how the team fits together, and how your kid feels on a random Tuesday in February, not just during the excitement of tryouts or the adjustment period of October. That makes one year in a genuinely useful moment to check in, honestly and without alarm, on how things are actually going.

A check-in like this isn't the same as looking for a problem. Most families who pause here find plenty going right alongside a few things worth a closer look, and that's a completely normal place to land. The goal is a clear picture, not a verdict.

02

Start With What's Actually Going Well

Before weighing any concerns, it's worth naming what a full season has actually built. Skating that used to look shaky now looks automatic. A kid who was nervous handling the puck under pressure now does it without thinking. Friendships that didn't exist last September are now the first thing your kid mentions after a game. None of that shows up on a scoresheet, but it's the real return on a demanding year.

Worth Noticing and Naming
  • ✓ Skills that were shaky in September are second nature now
  • ✓ Real friendships formed with teammates
  • ✓ Handles a tough game or a bad call better than a year ago
  • ✓ Still asks to go to open skate or watch hockey on off days
Worth a Closer Look
  • · Enthusiasm has dropped off compared to the start of the season
  • · Talks about wanting different teammates or a different coach
  • · Seems worn down more than excited most weeks

Most families find items in both columns, and that's expected. A season that's mostly working with one or two rough edges is a very different situation from one that feels wrong across the board, and it's worth being specific about which one you're actually looking at.

03

Ask Your Kid These Questions

The most reliable way to close out a season well is also the simplest: ask your kid directly, and actually listen to the answer instead of the one you're expecting. A lot of kids have a clearer, calmer read on their own season than parents give them credit for. A few questions, asked casually rather than as an interrogation, tend to open up a real conversation.

Looking Back
  • ✓ What was the best part of this season for you?
  • ✓ What's something you got better at that you're proud of?
  • ✓ Was there a teammate or coach who really helped you this year?
  • ✓ If you could keep one thing exactly the same next season, what would it be?
Looking Ahead
  • · Is there anything about practice or games you'd want different next year?
  • · What do you want to work on next season?
  • · Was there anything that made you dread going to the rink some days?
  • · Do you want to keep playing at this level, or is something else on your mind?

These conversations work best spread out over a few car rides rather than one sit-down talk. A question dropped casually on the way home from the rink tends to get a more honest answer than the same question asked at the kitchen table with everyone watching.

04

Know What Next Season Already Brings

Whatever came up in those conversations, it helps to know how your own association actually handles coaching from year to year before deciding anything needs to change on your end. Many programs hold tryouts every spring and rebuild teams from scratch each season, choosing a head coach only after the roster is set. In that kind of structure, a new coach and a new group of teammates are simply part of what next season brings, whether you do anything differently or not.

Worth Knowing Either Way

If your association reshuffles teams and coaches annually, a season that had some rough edges may look completely different next year without you having to do anything at all. If your association tends to keep the same coach with the same team over multiple years, and something did come up in your conversation with your kid, that's worth a direct, friendly conversation with the coach before the new season starts, just to close the loop.

If a fresh start at a different club ever does feel like the right move, it's more common than people expect and rarely as dramatic as it feels in the moment. Most associations have a straightforward release or transfer process, and switching between seasons rather than mid-season tends to go more smoothly for everyone, including your kid's current teammates.

05

Let Your Kid Help Choose What's Next

Whatever those car-ride conversations turned up, it's worth carrying your kid's own answers into whatever comes next, rather than deciding on their behalf and presenting it as settled. A kid who feels like they had a real say tends to feel good about wherever the season leads, whether that's coming back to the same team with a clear picture of what worked, or trying something a little different.

There's no wrong outcome here that a thoughtful conversation can't land well. Coming back for another season, some things adjusted, is just as good a result as a fresh start somewhere new. The goal was never a perfect season. It was raising a kid who still loves the game enough to want to lace up again next fall.
