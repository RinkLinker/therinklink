How High School Hockey Actually Works

School-sanctioned, club, prep, or academy, "high school hockey" means something different depending on where you live. Here's how the pieces actually fit together.

🏆 AAA & Elite · 7 min read · Built by hockey families
01

Four Different Things Can Be Called "High School Hockey"

As covered in our A vs AA vs AAA guide, high school hockey runs as its own parallel system, separate from club and travel hockey's tier structure. But "high school hockey" itself isn't one thing either. Depending on where you live, it can mean any of the following.

Type What It Means
School-sanctioned varsity Run by the state's high school athletic association, just like football or basketball
Club high school hockey USA Hockey-sanctioned, not officially tied to the school district, common where schools don't field their own teams
Prep school hockey A private boarding or day school's own varsity team, academics-first with serious hockey
Hockey academy Hockey-first, often with online schooling built around a heavy ice-time schedule

The first two are what most people mean by "high school hockey." The second two, prep schools and academies, are a different tier of decision entirely, closer in spirit to choosing an AAA program than to picking which local high school to attend.

02

Prep School Hockey: What It Actually Offers

Prep school hockey is essentially a private school's own varsity program, but with a level of coaching, ice time, and competition that regular public high school hockey usually can't match. New England prep schools in particular have long been one of the most productive pipelines in American hockey, Shattuck-St. Mary's alone has sent players like Sidney Crosby and Jonathan Toews through its program on the way to the NHL.

Academically, prep schools are generally a step up too, which is part of the appeal beyond hockey itself. Some New England prep leagues set firm standards, like requiring three-season sports participation, that regular high school or club programs don't.

Why Some Families Choose It Over Club Hockey

Midget AAA travel hockey can require missing 15 to 30 school days a year for tournaments and showcases, something many public and private schools have real problems accommodating. Prep schools tend to build that flexibility in from the start, since it's assumed and supported rather than treated as a disruption. It also cuts down dramatically on the driving, tournaments and travel that come with a AAA schedule, since the team and school are the same place.

03

Hockey Academies: A Different Trade-Off

Academies are a newer, more hockey-forward model than traditional prep schools. Instead of a full traditional academic campus, many run on a combination of online coursework, tutors, and academic advisors built around an intensive hockey schedule, sometimes stretching close to ten months a year. Some use a billeting model rather than dormitories.

This can be a strong fit for a family fully committed to the hockey path, but it's worth going in with clear eyes about the trade-off. If your kid has strong academic ambitions, it's worth directly researching how a specific academy's coursework is viewed by NCAA eligibility standards and by the colleges your kid might eventually apply to, rather than assuming all "school" programs are equivalent.

Worth Knowing

Prep is not the same as academy, even though they get lumped together in conversation. A traditional prep school is fundamentally an academic institution that also fields a strong hockey team. A hockey academy is fundamentally a hockey program that also provides schooling. Which one fits your family depends heavily on how you'd answer the question: if hockey stopped tomorrow, which environment would you still want your kid in?

04

The Transfer Question Nobody Quite Wants to Talk About

In some states, open enrollment policies technically let a student attend any public school in the state, not just their assigned district school. That naturally raises an obvious question in hockey circles: could a family use open enrollment purely to get their kid onto a stronger hockey program at another public school?

The honest answer is that this does happen, though the rules around it get complicated fast. It's generally against the rules for a coach to directly recruit an individual player to transfer, but schools can still promote their program publicly, and families do sometimes choose a school partly for its hockey team. Rules on transferring, including penalties like sitting out a season, vary significantly by state athletic association and can apply differently depending on whether a student is moving from public to private school or the other way around.

If you're considering a move like this for hockey reasons, the only reliable answer is to check your specific state's high school athletic association rules directly before assuming anything, since a transfer that's fine in one state's system could cost your kid a season of eligibility in another.

05

How Families Actually Decide

There's no universal right answer here, it depends on your kid's goals, your family's finances, and how strong the hockey option is in your local public school to begin with.

Worth Weighing
  • + Would your kid actually get real ice time, or sit behind recruited players?
  • + How strong is the academic program on its own merits?
  • + Is the cost genuinely sustainable for multiple years, not just one?
  • + Does your kid want this, separate from what you want for them?
Common Mistakes
  • ✕ Choosing based on a school's reputation without visiting or watching a practice
  • ✕ Assuming "prep" and "academy" mean the same thing
  • ✕ Deciding too late; prep and academy recruiting often starts by grade 8 or 9
  • ✕ Skipping the question of what happens if hockey doesn't work out

If prep or academy hockey is on the table, start the research early, admissions and hockey recruitment for these programs often move a full year ahead of enrollment, and attending a showcase or camp associated with a specific school is generally more useful than cold-emailing a coach with no in-person visibility.

Whichever version of high school hockey your family lands on, school-sanctioned, club, prep, or academy, the fit matters more than the label. Ask what a specific program actually looks like day to day before deciding based on its name.
