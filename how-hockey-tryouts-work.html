Travel & Competitive Hockey

How Hockey Tryouts Actually Work

From your kid's first placement skate to a AAA evaluation: what happens, what coaches are watching, and what your job is.

🏒 All Levels · 6 min read · Built by hockey families
01

What Happens at Every Tryout

No matter the level, the first few minutes look the same. You check in at a table near the rink entrance, usually run by a team manager or association volunteer, and your kid gets a number. That number gets pinned to the back of their jersey. It's how coaches identify players on the ice without knowing their names.

Then your kid goes to the locker room, gets dressed, and waits. That waiting period is where most of the pre-tryout anxiety lives. Your job is to get them there calm and on time. Everything else is out of your hands from that point.

On the ice, the structure varies by level, but most tryouts include skating drills (edges, crossovers, stops and starts), puck handling, passing and shooting, and some form of competitive play: small-area games or a full scrimmage. The competitive portion is usually where coaches make most of their decisions.

One Thing That Surprises First-Timers

Whether parents can watch depends entirely on the organization. Some let you stand at the glass. Others ask parents to leave the building. Ask ahead of time so you're not standing in a parking lot wondering what's happening.

02

What Coaches Are Actually Watching For

Most parents assume it's about skill. It's not only about skill. Skill gets a kid in the door, but it rarely decides the roster on its own. Here's what coaches are actually evaluating.

Skating
Edges, crossovers, acceleration, stops. Everything else in hockey builds on skating. A kid who can't skate well won't be able to keep up as the game gets faster. Coaches know it.
Evaluated First
Compete Level
Does your kid battle for pucks? Skate hard when they don't have it? Go to the corners? This matters more than scoring a goal in the scrimmage.
Evaluated First
Coachability
When a coach gives an instruction on the ice, does your kid listen and adjust? Coaches are building a team they'll spend a full season with. They notice who's easy to coach and who isn't.
Heavily Weighted
Response to Adversity
What happens after a missed pass or a bad shift? Does your kid shake it off and go hard, or do they sulk? Coaches watch this closely, especially at AA and above.
Heavily Weighted
Hockey Sense
Are they in the right place at the right time? Do they see the ice? This is harder to teach than skating, so coaches notice it when they see it, especially at AA and AAA.
Separates Levels
The Honest Truth

Coaches are also filling specific roster gaps: a defenseman, a big body, a kid who can kill penalties. Two equally skilled players can get different results based entirely on what the team needs. That's not politics. That's roster building.

03

What's Different at Each Level

The process changes significantly depending on whether your kid is trying out for in-house, travel, or AAA. Here's what to expect at each.

House League In-House Placement Skates

In-house tryouts aren't really tryouts. They're placement skates. Nobody gets cut. The goal is to sort kids onto balanced teams so no one team dominates the season. If this is your kid's first time on the ice in an evaluation, relax. They're going to make a team.

  • Usually one session, occasionally two
  • Parents almost always allowed to watch
  • Coaches evaluating basic skating, puck handling, and effort
  • Results posted within a few days. No dramatic phone call.
  • Low pressure, but a real evaluation. Effort still matters.
Travel A & AA Tryouts

This is where tryouts get real. Multiple sessions, and cuts happen after each one. This is often the first time a kid experiences being cut from something, and it's genuinely hard. Plan for that conversation before it happens.

  • Multiple sessions over 1–2 weeks
  • Numbers on jerseys. Coaches may not know your kid's name.
  • Parents often asked to stay off the glass or leave the building entirely
  • Cut notifications usually come by email or phone within 24–48 hours
  • Some organizations run tiered tryouts, sorting A and AA simultaneously
  • Team politics can start to appear at this level. You'll notice it more than your kid will.
AAA Elite Program Tryouts

AAA tryouts are more structured and more intense. Many programs use third-party evaluators so coaches aren't the only ones with input. At this level, showing up unknown is a real disadvantage. Coaches often already know most of the players from showcases and tournaments throughout the year.

  • Highly organized, sometimes run over multiple weekends
  • May require an invitation or pre-registration with limited spots
  • Third-party evaluators are common at legitimate programs
  • Visibility before tryouts matters. Relationships built during the season are part of the process.
  • Parents are almost never on the ice level
  • Some programs will tell you directly if your kid isn't a fit. Others won't.
What Comes Next Junior Camp Invitations

If your kid is playing at a high AAA level, junior teams may start reaching out about camps. This is exciting and confusing, and there is real money involved. Not all invitations are equal.

USHL and NAHL main camps run 120-200 players at $250-500 each. Teams send mass emails that read like personal invitations but aren't. A real invitation comes after a coach or scout has already seen your kid play and talked to their coaches directly. It sounds like a phone call, not an email blast.

  • USHL (Tier 1): no expenses at all — no tuition, housing covered by the team
  • NAHL (Tier 2): no tuition, but players typically pay $400-500/month for housing
  • Tier 3: players pay to play — understand what you're paying for before committing
  • Getting drafted or tendered means the team has invested in your kid. Attending a camp does not.
  • Before spending money on any camp, find out if a coach has actually seen your kid play and reached out personally
04

What Changes by Age Group

The level matters, but so does the age. Here's what's actually different as kids move through the system.

Age Group What's different at tryouts
Mites
Ages 6–8		 Barely a tryout. More of a first look. Coaches are watching who can skate forward without falling and who has basic puck awareness. Nobody's being evaluated on hockey IQ at this age.
Squirts
Ages 9–10		 First real cuts for travel kids. This is often a family's first experience with someone telling them their kid didn't make it. The gap between house and travel players starts to become visible here.
Peewee
Ages 11–12		 Checking gets introduced in most associations. Compete level becomes a much bigger factor. Coaches want to see how kids respond to contact and whether they'll initiate it. The physical gap between kids this age is wide.
Bantam
Ages 13–14		 It gets serious fast. At AAA and upper AA, some kids are already being looked at for prep schools and junior programs. Hockey sense and compete level matter as much as raw skill. Scouts occasionally appear.
Midget
Ages 15–18		 College recruiting is in the background for elite players. Juniors are a live conversation. Tryouts at the top levels feel closer to professional evaluations. Players are expected to show up ready. No grace period.
05

Your Job as the Parent

Tryout season is stressful for every hockey family. Here's how to actually help, and what to stay out of.

Before: Handle the Logistics
Make sure your kid has their gear, knows where to go, and gets there on time. Run through it the night before so tryout morning isn't chaotic. Beyond that, let them be nervous. Nerves are normal and they don't need you to fix it.
Your Job
During: Be a Calm Presence
If you're allowed to watch, watch quietly. Don't coach from the glass. Don't make faces. Don't text your spouse a play-by-play. Coaches notice parent behavior, and so do kids, even when they pretend not to.
Your Job
If You're Asked to Leave, Leave
Go get coffee. The tryout will go fine without you in the parking lot. Your anxiety in the lobby does not help your kid on the ice.
Your Job
The Car Ride Home
Ask how they felt about it, not how you thought it went. Let them lead. If they're quiet, let them be quiet. If they want to talk, listen more than you speak. The result comes later. That's a different conversation.
Most Important
If Your Kid Gets Cut

It's one of the harder things in youth hockey parenting. Let them feel it before you try to fix it. Read the full guide: My Kid Got Cut — Now What?
