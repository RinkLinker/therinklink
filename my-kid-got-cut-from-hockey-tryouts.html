Hockey Parent Survival

My Kid Got Cut. Now What?

One hockey mom's experience, and what coaches and sports psychologists say about helping your kid through one of the harder moments in youth sports.

💙 All Levels · 6 min read · Built by hockey families
01

The Moment Nobody Prepares You For

It is 10 o'clock on a school night. My kid is supposed to be sleeping. We are sitting somewhere in the house refreshing our phones, waiting for the list of numbers to show up , the ones that say who is moving on to the next set of tryouts. I was looking for one specific number. It was not on the list.

That is the moment. You start to question Did they have his number right? Who else made it through? Texts between parents start flashing and your heart just sinks, because now you have to walk down the hall and tell your kid he was cut on the first night.

My son started playing when he was eight, later than most, on an option team. He kept on improving. By the time he was trying out for Bantams he was pretty good. As a first-year Bantam we thought he had a real shot at AA. We found out he didn't by staring at a phone screen in the kitchen. Then we walked down the hall to tell him. He wasn't going to sleep. Neither were we.

He wasn't asleep. Neither were we. Waiting until morning felt wrong this time.

Worth Knowing

Sports psychologist Dr. Justin Anderson notes that making a team can feel so central to a young athlete's identity that not making it registers as genuine rejection, not just disappointment. "It's a really great microcosm for life," he says, "but you have to understand how much it can mean to them before you can help them through it."

Source: Dr. Justin Anderson, Premier Sport Psychology

02

That First Conversation

We walked into his room, sat down, and told him. Then we asked how he was feeling. Not what he thought happened. Not what he wanted to do next. Just how he was feeling right now.

Then we listened. That was basically it for that night. There was nothing to fix at 10pm on a school night, and trying to fix it would have made it about us. He needed to feel it first. The rest could come later.

A lot of hockey parents have been in that room. What tends to land well is simpler than you'd think: being present, asking an open question, and then staying quiet long enough for the answer to come. What tends to backfire is jumping too fast to explanations, solutions, or reassurances, even well-meaning ones.

Things That Tend to Help
  • + Asking how they're feeling and letting the answer come
  • + Sitting with the silence if that's what they need
  • + Saying you're proud of how hard they worked
  • + Letting them know you're not going anywhere
  • + Keeping the next morning as normal as possible
Things That Tend to Backfire
  • ✕ "The coaches don't know what they're doing"
  • ✕ Analyzing what went wrong on the ice that night
  • ✕ Telling them it's not a big deal
  • ✕ "Everything happens for a reason"
  • ✕ Calling the coach that night or the next morning
Why the Listening Matters

Parenting expert Sarah R. Moore puts it this way: let the emotions run their full course before moving into problem-solving. "Get curious, listen, and provide the child the experience of being heard." The instinct to fix things fast is natural, but moving too quickly past the feeling can leave kids without anywhere to put it.

Counselor and sports parent coach Rick Wolff adds that when his own kids got cut, he made a conscious effort to give them space first and then let them talk, let them vent, without needing to say much back. "Just be sincere," he says. "After the anger and tears subside for a day or two, then ask if they still want to play."

Source: Sarah R. Moore, author of Peaceful Discipline / Rick Wolff, AskCoachWolff.com

03

The Days After

They still have to go to school the next morning. That part is hard. The world doesn't pause because tryouts went sideways, and getting them up and out the door while they're carrying something heavy is its own kind of parenting. Keep the morning quiet. Normal helps.

In the days that follow, every kid processes differently. Some go quiet and need space. Some want to replay every detail. Some want to get back on the ice immediately and some need a week. Following their lead on the timing tends to work better than pushing a timeline for moving on.

The conversation about what's next, what team, what to work on, whether to try again, doesn't have to happen right away. A few days of just being your kid's parent, without the hockey agenda, can matter more than you'd expect.

Something Worth Considering

Red River Counseling suggests that one useful thing parents can help their kids figure out is what they actually lost. Often it isn't the hockey itself. It's the teammates, the identity, the status of being on that team. Helping a kid name what they're grieving specifically can make the grief easier to move through, rather than leaving it as this general feeling of rejection.

It's also worth noting that calling the coach in the heat of the moment rarely helps and sometimes makes things harder for your kid in the community long-term. If you want feedback on the evaluation, and it's reasonable to want that, most coaches respond better to a calm conversation a week or two later, framed around your kid's development rather than the decision.

04

Prove Them Wrong. Make Them Regret It.

This wasn't the first thing we said. It came a day or two later, when he had moved through the initial shock and started looking for somewhere to put the energy.

Prove them wrong. Make them regret they didn't pick you this season. That's what we told him. Not because we were bitter about the coaches, but because it gave him direction. Something to skate toward instead of away from.

The kids who come back from a cut and have a great season tend to be the ones who use it that way. They show up to the next practice differently. The season ahead of them becomes its own thing to play for, not just a consolation prize.

Maybe this is the year your kid gets more ice time than they would have gotten at the higher level. Maybe it's the year they become a leader on a team that actually needs them. Those things are real. They don't get talked about enough.

What the Research Says About Resilience

Sport psychologist Frank Smoll of the University of Washington notes that being cut isn't a failure. It's an opportunity to learn and improve, and that framing matters enormously for how kids move through it. The parents who help their kids land on that framing tend to produce athletes who come back stronger, not just athletes who survive the disappointment.

The key, according to Dr. Justin Anderson, starts with the parent's own acceptance of the outcome. Kids take a lot of cues from how we carry it. "If parents can rationally share in their child's disappointment and then turn it into an opportunity to help them cope with adversity, the child will learn to accept the result and move on in a positive way."

Source: Dr. Frank Smoll, University of Washington / Dr. Justin Anderson, Premier Sport Psychology

05

For the Parent Who Is Also Hurting

This part doesn't get talked about enough. When your kid gets cut, you get cut too. You have driven to the early morning practices, paid the fees, washed the gear, and sat in cold bleachers wanting this for them. That grief is real.

It just needs somewhere to go that isn't your kid's room at 10pm on the night it happened.

Finding another hockey parent who gets it, not to vent about the coaches, just to be understood by someone who has sat where you're sitting. It helps more than most people expect. This community exists. The parking lot after practice is full of parents who have been exactly here.

Giving yourself a day or two before having any conversation with coaches, other parents, or anyone in the hockey world about what happened also tends to be wise. Anything said from a place of fresh hurt has a way of following you around a community you'll be part of for years.

A Note on Your Own Feelings

Counselors who work with youth sports families often point out that when our kids don't make a team, our own unresolved feelings about rejection, fairness, and our kid's future can get tangled up in the moment. It doesn't mean the feelings aren't valid. It just means they're worth examining separately, so they don't land on your kid when they're already carrying something heavy.

06

They Kept Going Because They Loved It

Getting cut doesn't mean this is over. It means this particular team, this particular evaluator, this particular moment wasn't the right fit. That's a different thing entirely.

Some of the best players in hockey history were told no somewhere along the way. Not sharing this to suggest your kid is the next Hall of Famer. Just a reminder that an evaluator's opinion at 11 or 13 or 15 is not a final verdict on what your kid is capable of. The ones who made it kept going because the love of the game was bigger than the disappointment of the cut.

Martin St. Louis
Cut from his top team at 12U. Cut again at 14U. Not drafted by a single NHL team. He went on to a 16-year Hall of Fame career: more than 1,000 points, an MVP award, and a Stanley Cup. He has talked openly about what getting cut actually did for him: "Being cut makes you want things a little more. I really dominated the BB level and confidence-wise it was a big deal." He landed on a lower level, found his game, and built from there.
Joe Sakic
Didn't make his Midget A1 team. Stayed with the sport anyway. Eventually won the Stanley Cup with the Colorado Avalanche and became one of the most respected players in NHL history. The team that didn't take him probably thinks about that sometimes.
Brianne Jenner
One of the most decorated players in women's hockey, Olympic gold medalist and captain of the PWHL's Ottawa Charge, has spoken about being cut and using it as fuel. She kept going because she loved the game. That turned out to be enough.

Does your kid still love hockey? If yes, there is a season ahead of them and a team that needs them. The rest has a way of working itself out.
