Hockey Parent Guide

Hockey Lingo — Decoded

Everything you wish someone had handed you on the first day of hockey.

🏒 For New Hockey Families · ⏱ 5 min read · 📍 Colorado & Beyond
When my kid started playing hockey, I had no idea what anyone was talking about. Biscuits, barn burners, chirping — and that was just the first practice. I was Googling how to put on shin guards, whether to bring a blanket to the rink, and what half the terms even meant. I couldn't find one place with real answers for families like me — so I built it. This is everything I wish someone had handed me on day one.
01

The Rink

The Barn
What players call the rink. As in, "See you at the barn." You'll be saying it within a week.
The Ice / The Sheet
The playing surface. Hockey people don't say "the floor."
The Boards
The walls surrounding the ice. Players get checked into them — yes, on purpose.
The Glass
The plexiglass above the boards. Great for watching. Terrible for your eardrums.
💡 Parent Tip

Sit away from the glass during games — it's louder than you'd think.

The Crease
The blue painted area in front of each goal. Opposing players aren't supposed to be in it, and goalies are very protective of their space.
The Penalty Box
Where players sit after breaking a rule. Also called the sin bin.
⚠️ Heads Up

Your child will visit the penalty box. Stay calm. Cheer when they come out.

The Zamboni
The machine that resurfaces the ice between periods. Kids are obsessed with it. Adults secretly are too.
💡 Parent Tip

Zamboni breaks are your snack run and bathroom window. Use them wisely.

The Locker Room
Where teams gear up and bond. Parents almost always wait outside — this is intentional.
💡 Parent Tip

The locker room smell is real. There is nothing you can do about this. Accept it.

The Warm Room / Lobby
The heated area outside the rink. Your new best friend during 5am practices.
💡 Parent Tip

Dress in layers. The lobby is warm. The rink is not. Ever.

02

The Gear

The Bucket
The helmet. Every player wears one. Make sure it fits properly — this is not the place to size up for growth.
Cage vs. Visor
A cage is a full wire mask covering the face. A visor only covers the eyes. Youth hockey almost always requires a full cage.
⚠️ Watch Out

Check your association's rules before buying. Don't buy a visor by mistake.

Shin Guards
Protective pads worn under the socks, protecting the shins and knees. There is a correct order to put on all hockey equipment — and it matters.
💡 Parent Tip

The Rink Link has a full step-by-step dressing guide at therinklink.com/how-to-put-on-hockey-equipment — read it before the first practice. You'll thank yourself.

Breezers
Hockey pants. Called breezers in Colorado. Big, padded, and yes — they're supposed to look like that.
Skates
The most important piece of equipment. Get them properly fitted at a hockey specialty shop, not a department store.
⚠️ Watch Out

Skate sizes are NOT the same as shoe sizes — don't buy your kid's shoe size. Skates typically run 1 to 1.5 sizes smaller. Always get fitted in person.

The Stick
Comes in different flexes and lengths. The wrong flex makes shooting much harder for young players — ask your coach before buying.
The Bag
The massive hockey bag that will take over your car, your entryway, and eventually your entire life.
03

The Game

The Biscuit
The puck. "Keep your eye on the biscuit."
Icing
When a player shoots the puck all the way down the ice from behind center and nobody touches it. Play stops. It's a rule. Just trust it.
💡 Parent Tip

You will be confused by icing calls for most of your first season. Everyone is.

Offsides
A player can't enter the offensive zone before the puck crosses the blue line. Watch the blue line, not the player.
Power Play
When one team has more players on the ice because the other team has someone in the penalty box. Great time to watch — lots of action.
Hat Trick
Three goals scored by the same player in one game. If it happens, fans throw hats on the ice.
💡 Parent Tip

Keep a cheap hat in your bag just in case. It's a hockey tradition you'll want to participate in.

04

Player Slang

This is what your kid will start saying. Soon. Learn it now so you're not Googling it in the parking lot.

Flow
Long, glorious hockey hair flowing from under the helmet. A point of serious pride at every level.
"Check the flow on that kid."
Celly
Short for celebration — what a player does after scoring. Can range from a fist pump to a full choreographed routine.
"That celly was unreal."
Deke / Dangle
Fancy stickhandling to get around a defender. Your kid will practice these in the driveway starting immediately.
"He dangled through the whole defense."
Snipe
A perfectly placed, pinpoint-accurate shot. One of the highest compliments in hockey.
"What a snipe, bar down!"
Chirping
Trash talk on the ice. Part of the culture at every level — usually all in good fun.
"Those two were chirping all game."
Beauty
The highest compliment a hockey player can give. A great play, a great person, an incredible celly — all can be a beauty.
"He's an absolute beauty."
Barn Burner
A high-scoring, back-and-forth, wildly exciting game. You'll know one when you're in it.
"That third period was a barn burner."
Wheel
To skate fast — really fast. If your kid can wheel, everyone will know.
"She can wheel — nobody was catching her."
Sauce
A perfectly executed saucer pass — flat and airborne, landing right on a teammate's stick. Hard to do. Incredible to watch.
"That sauce was filthy."
Apple
An assist. Your kid got an apple means they helped set up a goal. Two apples in one game — treat accordingly.
"He had two apples tonight."
Light the Lamp
To score a goal. Refers to the red light that goes on behind the net when a goal is scored.
"She lit the lamp twice tonight."
Dirty / Greasy
A goal scored in a chaotic, scrappy way — usually in front of the net. Not pretty. Totally counts.
"That was a dirty goal but we'll take it."
Lettuce
Hair. Specifically great hockey hair. Often used interchangeably with flow.
"Look at that lettuce."
Mitts
Hands — specifically a player's stickhandling hands. "He's got mitts" means he can really handle the puck.
"Kid's got silky mitts."
Pipes
The goalposts. Hitting the pipe means the puck hit the post. Devastating when it's your team.
"Off the pipe and out. Unbelievable."
Breadwinner
The goal that wins the game. The most important goal scored.
"She potted the breadwinner with two minutes left."
Bender
A player whose ankles collapse inward when skating. Said affectionately — mostly. Also motivation to get properly fitted skates.
"Get that kid some new skates."
Gongshow
Total chaos — on or off the ice. A wild, unruly situation. Can be used admiringly.
"That third period was an absolute gongshow."
💡 The Simple Version

When your kid starts using these words at home, it means they're becoming a real hockey player. Embrace it. Maybe even try one yourself — they'll either love it or be mortified. Both are a win.

05

Parent Survival Guide

5am Ice
Early morning practice slots, often before school. It's exactly as brutal as it sounds.
💡 Parent Tip

Set two alarms. Lay out all the gear the night before. Every single time.

Hockey Time
Everything in hockey takes longer than scheduled. Practice runs late. Games run late. Parking lots are slow.
💡 Parent Tip

Add 30 minutes to everything on your calendar. You'll still occasionally be late.

Dry Land
Off-ice training — conditioning, skating drills in shoes, stickhandling practice. Just as important as ice time.
House vs. Travel
House (rec) hockey stays local with manageable schedules. Travel hockey involves tournaments, road trips, and much higher costs.
💡 Parent Tip

Both are great. Know what fits your family's life before committing — the jump from house to travel is significant.

Tournament Weekend
Multi-game events held Friday through Sunday, often at multiple rinks across the region.
💡 Parent Tip

Pack snacks, a blanket, a charger, patience, and more snacks. You will be at a rink all weekend.

Stick Tap
Players tap their sticks on the ice to show respect for a great play or acknowledge teammates. One of the best traditions in the sport.
The Hockey Family
The community of parents, coaches, and kids you'll spend your weekends with for years. They become your people.
💡 Parent Tip

Lean into the community early. The friendships you build in hockey rink lobbies are real ones.

06

Colorado Hockey

CAHA — Colorado Amateur Hockey Association
The governing body for amateur hockey in Colorado. Your team registers through them each season.
USA Hockey
The national governing body. Every player needs a USA Hockey registration number before stepping on the ice.
💡 Parent Tip

Register early at usahockey.com — you can't skate without it.

Altitude
Colorado's elevation means players may tire more quickly, especially opponents from lower elevations. Your home team is used to it — visiting teams are not.
House Level / Rec Hockey
Local-league hockey with no tryouts and everyone plays. A great starting point for most families new to the sport.
