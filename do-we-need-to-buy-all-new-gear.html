New to Hockey

Do We Need to Buy All New Gear?

A full kit can run $700 before your kid steps on the ice once. Here's what actually needs to be new, what's smart to buy used, and where hockey families find both.

🏒 All Levels · 6 min read · Built by hockey families
01

The Sticker Shock Is Real

A complete new youth hockey kit runs somewhere between $400 and $700 at retail, covering the basics: helmet, neck guard, shoulder pads, elbow pads, gloves, shin guards, pants, skates, and a stick. Skates and the helmet tend to be the two biggest single-line items, often $80 to $150 each for entry-level gear that actually fits and protects properly.

Compare that to what a youth soccer registration includes, a jersey and a ball are usually part of the fee, and hockey starts to feel like a different category of sport entirely. It is. But the good news is that the second-hand market for youth hockey gear is deep, active, and full of quality equipment, because kids outgrow gear faster than they wear it out.

The Real Math

A used full set typically runs $50 to $200, roughly a quarter of retail. Most hockey associations run equipment exchanges or gear banks specifically because so many families are cycling through sizes every season. This isn't a fringe workaround. It's how a large share of hockey families actually outfit their kids, especially in the younger age groups.

02

The Option Most Families Don't Know About

Before buying or borrowing anything, check whether your NHL team runs a Learn to Play program. In Colorado, that's the Avalanche's Mile High Mites, run in partnership with the NHL and NHLPA through local associations and rinks across the state. For a single flat fee, generally $250 to $305, a first-time player gets a full set of brand-new, properly fitted gear, skates, helmet, gloves, shoulder pads, shin guards, elbow pads, pants, stick, jersey, and an equipment bag, plus around six on-ice lessons to go with it.

This isn't a rental. The gear is the player's to keep, and it's sized and fitted specifically for them rather than handed down from someone else's kid. Most NHL teams run a version of this same program nationally, so it's worth a quick search for your team plus "Learn to Play" even outside Colorado. The program is generally reserved for players not currently rostered on a team, so it works best as the true starting point, before your kid joins a house or travel program, not as a mid-season top-up.

Worth Knowing

These programs exist specifically to lower the barrier to entry for new hockey families, and the math is hard to beat. A single fee that's less than the cost of skates and a helmet alone at retail gets a first-time player everything they need, correctly fitted, plus real instruction. If your kid hasn't played before, this is worth checking before spending anything else on gear.

03

What's Worth Buying New

Not everything is created equal here. A few pieces protect against real impact, mold to your kid's body over time, or degrade in ways that aren't visible from the outside. Those are the ones worth the extra cost.

Gloves Buy New
Goalie pads, blocker, catcher (if goaltending) Depends on Age
Skates Either Works
Stick Used Is Smart
Shin guards, elbow pads, shoulder pads, pants Used Is Fine

Helmets take direct impact and their internal foam can degrade or crack in ways you can't see from the outside, which is exactly why most manufacturers and safety organizations recommend buying new. Gloves get a similar recommendation, mostly because they take a beating and a well-fitted new pair actually protects hands better than a broken-in used one that's lost its shape.

Goalie equipment gets an asterisk. For a young, growing goalie, the same logic that applies to skates tends to apply here too, kids usually outgrow pads and a blocker before they've put enough hours on the ice to actually wear the gear out. A lot of "used" youth goalie equipment on the market is really just lightly used gear that got sized out of, not gear that took years of abuse. That makes it a genuinely good option for younger goalies, provided you inspect it the way you would any used gear.

The calculus shifts as a goalie gets older, plays more, and starts logging serious ice time, at that point, gear is more likely to have real wear, and hidden structural damage or compressed foam becomes a more realistic risk. For an older or more advanced goalie playing heavy minutes, buying new starts to make more sense. If new isn't realistic at that stage, at minimum have used gear inspected closely, and know you may need to replace it sooner than you would with new equipment.

Worth Knowing

Skates are the one everyone assumes has to be new, and it's often the easiest place to save. There's little competitive difference for a young player between broken-in used skates and a stiff new pair, and used skates are actually more pliable and comfortable right away. Save the higher-end new pair for when your kid's feet stop growing every few months.

04

Where Hockey Families Actually Find It

Start with your local hockey association before you start with a retail store. Many associations run rental programs for brand-new players, sometimes free or close to it, specifically so families can try the sport before committing to a full purchase. Ask about this before buying anything.

From there, most associations also run seasonal equipment exchanges or maintain a gear bank, essentially a supply of donated or traded gear sorted by size. Regional hockey Facebook groups and local classifieds serve the same purpose and tend to move fast, since growing kids create a steady churn of gently used sizes.

Good Signs on Used Gear
  • + Straps and buckles fully intact
  • + Padding still has bounce, not flattened
  • + No cracks in hard plastic shells
  • + No lingering odor after a wash
Reasons to Pass
  • ✕ Visible cracks in helmet shell
  • ✕ Flattened, compressed foam padding
  • ✕ Torn liner or broken chin strap
  • ✕ Goalie gear with heavy, high-level game use behind it

If neither hand-me-downs nor a local exchange turns up what you need, a starter kit is a reasonable middle ground. These bundle most of the protective basics, shin guards, elbow pads, shoulder pads, and pants, into one purchase, typically $125 to $250. You'll still need to source a helmet, gloves, skates, and a stick separately.

05

One Thing Worth Getting Right

Fit matters more than price tag, especially for skates. A pair that's a size too big to "grow into" will hurt your kid's skating more than any gear brand or price point will help it. If you can get to a hockey shop for even a basic fitting once, before you buy anything, new or used, it's worth the trip.

Beyond that, the stick genuinely doesn't make the player at this stage. A lower-cost stick performs just as well for a beginner as an expensive one, and kids will outgrow the correct flex rating long before they wear a stick out. This is one of the easiest places to save without any real tradeoff.

Most of what your kid needs to get on the ice this season is probably sitting in another hockey family's garage right now. Ask around before you buy anything new.
