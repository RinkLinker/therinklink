How to Choose a Private Hockey Coach in Colorado

Everything your family needs to know before booking that first session — from what to look for to the questions you should always ask.

Colorado ⏱ 5 min read ️ Built by hockey families
00

Does Your Child Actually Want This?

Before you spend a dollar on private coaching, ask yourself one honest question: is this your dream or your child's? It's one of the most important things to get right — and one of the easiest to get wrong when you love the sport and want the best for your kid.

Private coaching requires real commitment — early morning ice times, extra practice outside of team sessions, and a player who is mentally engaged, not just physically present. A reluctant player won't get much out of it, no matter how good the coach is.

Here's how to check if your child is genuinely ready:

  • They ask about it unprompted
    "Can I get extra skating lessons?" is a very different starting point than "Do I have to go?"
  • They show frustration when they cannot do something
    That frustration is actually a good sign. It means they care enough to want to improve.
  • They watch hockey on their own
    Kids who love the game consume it. They study players, replay goals, talk about the sport constantly.
  • They push through hard practices without shutting down
    Private sessions are more demanding than team practice. Mental toughness matters.
  • They respond well to correction
    A coachable kid who accepts feedback will get 10x more out of private sessions than one who shuts down when corrected.
️Honest Talk

If your child is under 8 or 9, don't stress too much about readiness — at that age, fun IS the goal. Private coaching for young kids works best when it feels like play, not pressure. Save the serious development conversation for when they're older and asking for it themselves.

The Best Sign of All

Your kid comes home from a private session and immediately wants to go back. That's the clearest green light you'll ever get. Everything else is secondary.

01

Know What You're Actually Looking For

Private hockey coaching isn't one-size-fits-all. Before you start searching, get clear on what your player actually needs right now — because the right coach for a 7-year-old learning to skate is a completely different person than the right coach for a 16-year-old trying to make a AAA team.

  • Skating fundamentals — edges, crossovers, stops, backward skating. Most players need more of this than they think.
  • Puck handling & stickwork — individual skill development away from team practice.
  • Shooting & scoring — shot mechanics, release, accuracy under pressure.
  • Positional coaching — reading the ice, decision-making, defensive zone coverage.
  • Goalie-specific training — a completely different specialty. Don't hire a skater coach for a goalie.
Pro Tip

Ask your team coach where your player's biggest gap is before hiring a private coach. Their answer might surprise you — and it's the most honest starting point you'll get.

02

Credentials Matter, But Experience Matters More

Colorado has a lot of coaches. Some have impressive certifications, some played at high levels, and some are just really good at teaching kids. The best coaches often have all three — but when you have to choose, prioritize someone who has a proven track record of developing players at your child's level.

Look for coaches who have:

  • USA Hockey certification — the baseline credential. Level 1 is entry-level; Level 4 or higher signals serious commitment to coaching development.
  • Experience with your player's age group — coaching mites is very different from coaching midgets.
  • References from current or former players' families — real testimonials beat any credential.
  • A clear methodology — they should be able to explain how they develop players, not just list what they've done.
03

The Questions You Should Always Ask

A good coach will welcome these questions. A bad one will get defensive. Use that reaction as data.

01
What's your coaching philosophy for a player at my kid's level? You want to hear something specific, not a generic answer about "fundamentals."
02
Can I watch the first session before committing to a package? Most good coaches say yes. It's a red flag if they say no.
03
How do you communicate progress with parents? You should get regular feedback, not silence between sessions.
04
Do you have references from families whose kids are at a similar level? Ask for two or three and actually follow up with them.
05
What rinks do you work out of, and how do ice times work? Understand logistics before you commit — early morning ice at a rink 45 minutes away adds up fast.
06
What's your cancellation policy? Life happens. Know the rules before you need them.
04

What to Expect to Pay in Colorado

Private coaching rates in Colorado vary widely depending on experience level, session format, and location. Here's a general range to help you calibrate:

  • $50–80/hour — newer coaches, group sessions, or coaches building their client base.
  • $80–120/hour — experienced coaches with a solid track record and established roster.
  • $120–200+/hour — elite development coaches, former pros, or specialists with long waitlists.
Pro Tip

Some coaches offer small group sessions (2–4 players) at a lower per-player rate. If your player has a teammate at a similar level, this can cut costs significantly while keeping the quality high.

Don't automatically assume the most expensive coach is the best fit for your player. A $70/hour coach who specializes in your kid's age group and communicates well with parents may deliver better results than a $150/hour ex-pro who runs cookie-cutter sessions.

05

Red Flags to Watch Out For

Most coaches in Colorado are great. But here are signs to walk away from:

  • Won't let you observe a session before committing to a package
  • Pressures you to buy a large session package upfront before you've seen them coach
  • Can't clearly explain what they're working on or why
  • Never gives you feedback after sessions
  • Dismisses your player's team coach or badmouths other coaches
  • No references, no online presence, no verifiable background
  • Inconsistent scheduling or frequent last-minute cancellations
️Important

Always verify that any coach working with minors has a current USA Hockey SafeSport certification. This is non-negotiable and any legitimate coach will have it readily available.

06

How to Know It's Working

Private coaching is an investment. Here's how to know it's paying off:

  • Your player is excited to go to sessions, not dreading them
  • You can see specific, measurable improvements — not just vague "getting better"
  • The coach gives you concrete feedback after each session
  • Your team coach notices changes at practice
  • Your player is applying what they learn in games, not just in drills

If you're three months in and can't point to anything specific that's improved, it's okay to have that conversation with the coach — or to try someone new. Good coaches welcome the accountability.

07

How Old Should My Child Be Before Starting?

There is no magic age — but there are useful guidelines based on what kids are developmentally ready for at each stage.

  • Ages 4–6 (Learn to Skate)
    Focus entirely on fun and basic balance. Group Learn to Skate programs are perfect here. Private coaching at this age is rarely necessary and can create pressure that backfires.
  • Ages 7–9 (Mite level)
    This is when private skating lessons start to make sense — especially if your child is passionate. Keep it light, skill-focused, and fun. One session every week or two is plenty.
  • Ages 10–12 (Squirt/Peewee)
    The sweet spot for starting private coaching. Kids are coachable, can focus for a full session, and improvements show up quickly on the ice.
  • Ages 13–15 (Bantam)
    Players who want to move up competitively often add private coaching here. Focus shifts from pure skill to hockey IQ, positioning, and compete level.
  • Ages 16+ (Midget/high school)
    Players serious about juniors or college hockey are often working with positional specialists and skating coaches. At the top tiers, private coaching is almost expected.
Bottom Line

Earlier is not always better. A motivated 10-year-old will get far more out of private coaching than a burned-out 8-year-old whose parents pushed them into it too soon.

08

Private Sessions vs. Group Training

Private one-on-one coaching is not the only option — and for many families, it is not the best starting point. Here is how to think about the tradeoffs:

Private Sessions
  • + 100% focused on your player
  • + Coach adapts in real time
  • + Fastest path to fixing specific weaknesses
  • – More expensive per hour
  • – No competitive element with peers
Group Training
  • + Lower cost per player
  • + Competitive energy pushes players harder
  • + Great for general skill-building
  • – Less individual attention
  • – Cannot target your child's specific gaps

A smart approach many families use: start with group training to build a foundation and gauge your child's interest, then add private sessions once you know exactly what they need to work on.

Budget Tip

Look for small group sessions with 2–4 players. Many coaches offer them, and the per-player cost is significantly lower while still getting more individual attention than a full group clinic.
