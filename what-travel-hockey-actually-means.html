Travel Teams

What Travel Hockey Actually Means

Your kid made a team, or is about to try. Here's what actually changes, before the season starts asking things of your whole family.

🏒 Travel Teams · 6 min read · Built by hockey families
01

What You're Saying Yes To

The word "travel" undersells it. What you're actually signing up for is a different rhythm to your entire week, not just an upgrade from one or two ice sessions to three or four. Practices get more structured, games get more consequential, and weekends stop being reliably yours.

Tournaments are where this becomes real for most families. Where house league mostly stays inside your own rink and maybe one neighboring town, travel means driving an hour or two on a regular basis, and a handful of weekends a season built around a hotel, a strange rink, and games stacked back to back. Some clubs travel further than others, and that distance is worth asking about directly before you commit, since it varies enormously by club and level.

None of this is a warning against doing it. Plenty of families find that rhythm energizing rather than draining, especially once everyone settles into it. But it is a real shift, and going in with an accurate picture beats being surprised by your third tournament weekend in October.

02

The Levels Aren't One Thing

"Travel hockey" isn't a single tier. It's a ladder, usually labeled B, A, AA, and AAA, with real gaps in competitiveness, cost, and time commitment between each rung. Some associations also run B teams as a second, less competitive travel option below A, or as a club's second team at a given age group, so don't assume "travel" automatically means the top tier your kid could reach. A-level often looks like a gentler step up from house league, still local-ish, still manageable. AAA looks almost nothing like house league at all, closer to a part-time job for the whole family.

Where your kid lands isn't fixed the moment they make a travel roster. Movement between levels happens constantly, in both directions, based on tryouts each year. We've written a fuller breakdown of what separates A from AA from AAA if you want the details before your family picks a lane.

03

Tryouts Become an Annual Event

This is the part that catches families off guard who are new to travel: making the team this year doesn't guarantee a spot next year. Nearly every travel program re-evaluates its roster annually, turning the tryout your kid just survived into a yearly ritual for as long as they play at this level.

Timing catches families off guard too. Travel tryouts typically happen in spring, often April or May, for the season that starts the following fall. If you're reading this in August wondering whether your kid can still try out for travel this year, the honest answer is probably not, at least not through the normal process. Mark next spring on the calendar now if that's the direction your family is headed.

If your kid hasn't been through a real travel tryout yet, our tryout guide and how tryouts work guide walk through what coaches are actually watching for. And if it doesn't go the way your kid hoped, we've written honestly about that too: My Kid Got Cut, Now What?

04

The Trade You're Making

More ice time and more competition come with a tradeoff worth naming honestly: less room for other sports. A lot of travel schedules, especially at AA and AAA, push toward hockey nearly year-round, and that pull toward specializing early is worth understanding rather than drifting into without thinking about it.

What the Research Actually Says

Sports medicine research on early single-sport specialization has been fairly consistent for over a decade now. Athletes who specialize before age 12, meaning close to year-round training in one sport, show meaningfully higher burnout rates than those who wait until their mid-teens, and specialization is linked to higher rates of overuse injury as well, not just in hockey but across youth sports generally.

That doesn't mean travel hockey is a mistake, or that your kid needs to quit soccer to succeed. It means the families who do well with a heavy travel schedule tend to build in real off-time on purpose, whether that's a full off-season, a second sport, or simply weeks on the calendar with no ice at all.

Source: Sports medicine research on youth sport specialization, including findings summarized by UCLA Health and peer-reviewed injury prevention studies

Money is part of this trade too, and it's a big enough topic that it deserves its own honest breakdown rather than a paragraph here. We're working on a full guide to what travel hockey actually costs, since the number families budget for going in is rarely the number they end up spending by March.

05

Is This Right for Your Family

Every family that's made this jump successfully seems to agree on one thing: it works best when the kid wants it, not just the parent. If you're still weighing whether your family is ready for this step at all, our guide on House vs. Travel: Do We Need to Move Up? walks through that decision directly.

If you've already decided and you're just trying to understand what's ahead, the honest answer is that travel hockey asks more of everyone in the house, not just the kid on the ice. What it gives back, for the families who find the right fit, tends to be worth it.
