Pack Your Own Bag

You're old enough to do this yourself. Here's exactly what goes in your bag — and how to never forget anything again.

🏒 Ages 8–10 · Mites & Squirts · Built by hockey families

Here's the deal: good hockey players take care of their own stuff. That means knowing what goes in your bag, checking it yourself the night before, and never making your mom or dad run back inside because you forgot your helmet.

This is your list. Learn it. Use it. Own it.

01

The Gear That Goes In Every Time

Every practice. Every game. Every single time. No exceptions.

⛸️ Skates
Check that your laces aren't fraying. If they look rough, tell a parent before game day — not in the parking lot.
⛑️ Helmet
With your cage or shield attached. No helmet = no skating. Don't be that kid.
🛡️ Neck Guard
It's required. It protects you. Wear it every time, no arguments.
💪 Shoulder Pads
Make sure nothing feels loose or broken. Tell a parent if something feels off.
🦾 Elbow Pads
Both of them. Yes, both.
🧤 Gloves
Both of them. Same rule.
🦵 Shin Guards
Tuck them inside your hockey socks. If they're sliding around, use sock tape.
🏒 Hockey Pants
Also called breezers. They protect your hips and tailbone. Always in the bag.
🩲 Jock or Jill
Always. No exceptions. You know why.
🧦 Hockey Socks
Go over your shin guards. Use sock tape at the top to keep them up.
👕 Jersey
Team jersey for games, practice jersey for practice. Know which one you need before you pack.
🏒 Stick
Lean it by the front door the night before so you can't leave without it.
😁 Mouthguard
Required. Keep a spare in your bag at all times. They're cheap. Teeth are not.
💧 Water Bottle
Filled. With your name on it. Don't share. Don't show up without one.
🌙
Pack the night before. Every time.

If you pack your bag the night before practice or a game, you will never forget anything. Ever. This is the one habit that separates prepared players from the ones still looking for their glove in the parking lot at 5:50am.

02

Smart Extras to Keep in Your Bag

These things live in your bag permanently. Once they're in there, leave them there.

Extra Skate Laces
One pair, same size as yours. Laces snap right before games. It happens. Be ready.
Sock Tape
Keeps your shin guard socks up. Keep a roll in the front pocket of your bag.
Skate Guards
Put them on when you walk to and from the ice. Take them off before you step on. Every single time.
Small Towel
Wipe your blades dry after skating. Wet blades rust. Rust ruins edges. Dry blades = sharp blades.
Spare Mouthguard
Keep one in a small bag in your hockey bag. The one you lost is always the only one you had.
03

After Practice — Your Job

Packing the bag is only half of it. What you do after practice matters just as much.

Dry your skate blades
Use your towel. Takes 10 seconds. Keeps your edges sharp and your blades rust-free.
Put your skate guards on
Before you walk anywhere off the ice. Protect those blades.
Air out your gear at home
Don't leave wet gear zipped in your bag overnight. Hang it up or lay it out. Gear that dries smells way better. Trust us.
Tell a parent if anything is broken or missing
Broken lace, cracked strap, lost mouthguard — tell them that night, not in the car on the way to the rink.
Repack for next time before you go to bed
This is the move. Gear goes back in the bag the same night. Next morning is easy. No stress. Just skate.
Real Talk

The players who take care of their gear take care of their game. It's not just about being organized — it's about being the kind of player who shows up ready. Coaches notice that stuff. Teammates notice it too.

04

The Things Players Forget Most

These are the most common things that get left behind. Don't be that player.

The Stick
Left leaning against the wall by the garage. Fix: it goes in the bag or next to the front door. Nowhere else.
The Helmet
Left airing out on the shelf. Fix: it goes back in the bag after it dries, not back on the shelf.
The Water Bottle
Still in the dishwasher or on the counter. Fix: water bottle lives in the bag. Period.
The Mouthguard
On the bathroom counter in its little case. Fix: keep a spare in the bag at all times. The spare becomes the main one the day you lose the original.
One Glove
Only one. Don't ask how it happens. It just does. Always count: two gloves, two shin guards, two elbow pads.
🏆
You've Got This

Packing your own bag is a small thing that makes a big difference. Every time you do it yourself, you're becoming a better teammate, a more prepared player, and a little more independent. Your parents will notice. Your coaches will notice. And honestly? It feels pretty good.
