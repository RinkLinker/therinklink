How Much Does House League Actually Cost?

Registration, equipment, the small recurring costs, and why it's still one of the best values in youth sports.

🏒 House League · 6 min read · Built by hockey families
01

Still One of the Best Values Around

House league sits well below every other level of hockey on cost, and it's not close. A full season nationally runs about $1,400 to $4,100 all-in, covering registration, equipment, and the light travel a house league schedule actually requires. Compare that to a used bike and a season of club soccer, and hockey holds up better than its reputation suggests.

One note on methodology: the range above comes from Hockey Budget, a consumer cost-tracking site that surveys registration fees, gear costs, and travel expenses by state and level each season. It's not peer-reviewed research, and your own club's numbers may land higher or lower depending on your region. Treat it as a realistic planning range, not an exact quote.

02

Where the Registration Fee Actually Goes

Registration is the single biggest line item at this level, typically starting around $600 to $850 a season in hockey-dense states and running higher elsewhere. That fee covers ice time, USA Hockey membership, and coaching, which at house league is usually a volunteer parent rather than a paid staff member.

Cost Category Typical Range
Registration $600 – $1,600
Equipment (full set) $270 – $1,080
Travel and gas $150 – $400
Concessions and small extras $150 – $500

Practice frequency at house league stays light compared to travel, usually one or two sessions a week plus a game on the weekend, which is exactly why the ice-time portion of the fee stays manageable.

03

Equipment Is the Real Upfront Number

The bigger sticker shock usually isn't registration, it's the first full equipment set. Costs scale with age and size, since older kids need bigger, more protective gear across the board.

Mite Age (8U)
  • · Full set: $270 – $520
  • · Smaller pads, lower price per piece
Squirt/Peewee (10U–12U)
  • · Full set: $430 – $1,080
  • · Bigger gear, more frequent replacement as they grow

Bantam and Midget-age players (14U to 18U) run $650 to $1,800 for a full set, since gear at that size approaches adult pricing. The good news at house league specifically: gear swap events and club trade-in programs are common, and buying used cuts these numbers substantially, especially for skates that get outgrown every season regardless of how well they're cared for.

04

The Small Costs That Add Up Anyway

Even at house league, a handful of small recurring costs sneak past most family budgets. A team photo, a coach gift, and an end-of-season pizza party are close to universal, usually landing somewhere between $50 and $150 total for the season. Concessions at the rink add up the same way they do at every level, a few dollars a visit that becomes real money by March.

What's Genuinely Minimal at This Level

Travel is the category that separates house league from every level above it. Most house teams play within a thirty-minute drive, meaning gas costs stay close to $150 for the full season rather than the $1,000-plus that travel families budget for tournament weekends. This is the single biggest reason house league costs a fraction of what travel does.

05

Location Still Changes the Number

Hockey-dense states keep house league costs low because ice time is more available and less expensive. Minnesota house league registration can run as low as $1,400 all-in, while the same level in California or Hawaii can run closer to $4,100, mostly due to higher ice rental rates and less local competition among rinks.

A few things help regardless of region. Ask your club directly about payment plans, which most offer even without advertising them. Check whether your state has grant programs through USA Hockey's foundation or organizations like Canadian Tire Jumpstart if you're in Canada. And buy used where you can, particularly skates, while still making sure everything actually fits.

Whatever the number ends up being, house league remains one of the most affordable ways for a kid to build real skill, real friendships, and a real love of the game. Plenty of families stay here for years, and there's nothing about that number that should feel like a compromise.
