When Do Players Start Getting Noticed?

Bantam, Midget, and everything scouts actually look at. A realistic breakdown of when the recruiting conversation actually starts, and what it looks like when it does.

🏆 AAA & Elite · 7 min read · Built by hockey families
01

The Honest Starting Point

For the vast majority of players, real recruiting conversations don't start until well into the teenage years, and this isn't just a general impression, it's backed by both the NCAA's own rules and its own data. Bantam, the 13 to 14 age group, is where most families first start thinking seriously about the NCAA or junior hockey path, even if nothing concrete happens yet.

The Official Rule

College Hockey Inc., the recruiting resource jointly run by the NCAA and USA Hockey, states plainly in its own FAQ: Division I college hockey coaches are not allowed to initiate contact with prospective student-athletes until June 15 of a recruit's sophomore year of high school, roughly age 15 to 16. Before that date, coaches legally cannot reply to emails, texts, or phone calls from a recruit, no matter how good that player is.

Source: College Hockey Inc., official NCAA/USA Hockey recruiting FAQ

College Hockey Inc. also surveyed its own NCAA Division I player population ahead of the 2025-26 season, roughly 250 players across more than two dozen schools, and found the average commitment age was over 17 years old. That's a meaningfully later number than the "get noticed by 13" narrative that circulates in youth hockey circles, straight from the organization that runs the recruiting rules itself.

Age Group What's Typically Happening
Peewee (11-12) Rare, informal attention on standout players only
Bantam (13-14) Informal evaluation begins at tournaments and camps; no official coach contact permitted yet
Minor Midget (15-16) June 15 of sophomore year: official contact and verbal offers become legally permitted
Major Midget / Junior (16-20) Heaviest scouting window; average NCAA D-I commitment age is 17+

That said, "no official contact" doesn't mean "no evaluation." College Hockey Inc. and recruiting services both note that coaches attend tournaments, camps, and combines to evaluate talent as young as 8th grade, and may contact a recruit's club or high school coach for informal feedback well before that June 15 date. The formal contact rule governs direct communication with the player, not whether anyone's watching.

02

What "Getting Noticed" Actually Looks Like

It rarely looks like a single dramatic moment. Scouts and coaches attend showcase tournaments, watch AAA league games, and increasingly rely on video and stat-tracking platforms that follow players across seasons and leagues. A player's profile on a site like Elite Prospects has become something close to a hockey resume, tracked and compared well before any formal offer conversation happens.

Standout players occasionally get early attention. Some of the game's biggest names were known to scouts by 13, but that's the exception, not a typical timeline, and it doesn't mean a player who isn't drawing that kind of notice at 13 has missed anything. For players in Western Canada and Minnesota westward, the WHL Bantam Draft is a formal early entry point, teams scout second-year Bantams throughout the season and draft eligible players each spring, though this is specific to that region and league.

Worth Knowing

Most key NCAA and junior evaluations happen between ages 16 and 20, once players are competing in junior leagues or high-level Midget hockey. The players who reach that stage well-positioned are typically the ones who kept developing steadily, not the ones who were identified earliest. Early notice can open doors, but it's the years of development after that actually determine where a player ends up.

03

The Tradeoffs of Chasing Early Exposure

The average NCAA commitment age is over 17, per College Hockey Inc.'s own player survey, but the extreme early end of that range has genuinely crept younger in recent years. A small number of high-profile commitments now happen at 14 or 15, early enough that it was part of the reason the NCAA revisited its eligibility rules in 2026, discussed in our guide on the recent NCAA changes. That's still the exception, not the norm reflected in the 17+ average, and it's worth keeping that distinction in mind before assuming it's expected.

Families chasing early exposure often end up committing to more travel, more showcase tournaments, and a heavier financial investment, all in pursuit of visibility that may not be necessary for a player who's still developing at a normal pace. It can also mean less multi-sport participation and more year-round hockey at an age where physical and skill development benefits from variety, not specialization.

Something Worth Considering

Chasing exposure too early can put pressure on a player and a family before it's actually useful. A kid who's still growing into their body, their game, and their love of the sport doesn't need to be seen by everyone at 13. What tends to matter more in the long run is steady development, staying healthy, and staying in a program where the player is actually getting real ice time, not just a name on an elite roster.

04

What Actually Helps a Player Get Seen

Playing meaningful minutes at the highest level a kid can genuinely compete at tends to matter more than chasing every showcase on the calendar. Scouts are looking for players who perform consistently across games and seasons, not just a highlight reel from one strong tournament.

Video matters more than it used to. Games are recorded more than ever, and profiles on tracking platforms accumulate over years, not weeks. That means consistency across a full season carries more weight than any single standout performance. For the specifics of what actually belongs in a highlight video and what to track, see our Video, Stats, and Getting Seen guide.

For most families, there's no clock running out at 13. The path to being noticed runs through steady development over years, not a single moment of discovery. Let the timeline unfold at your kid's pace.
