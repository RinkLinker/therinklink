Skate & Stick Sizing Guide

How to size skates and sticks correctly — and how to adjust as your player grows.

⛸️ Skates & Sticks · ⏱ 7 min read · 🧒 All Youth Ages
01

Hockey Skate Sizing & Fit

Skate fit is one of the most important — and most misunderstood — parts of youth hockey. Skates that don't fit correctly cause blisters, ankle instability, and frustration on the ice. Getting this right is worth the extra effort.

Skate Sizing vs. Shoe Sizing

Hockey skates do not follow regular shoe sizes. Most youth skates run 1 to 1.5 sizes smaller than a regular shoe. A child wearing a size 4 shoe typically wears a size 2.5–3 skate. Always measure the actual foot length, not shoe size.

⚠️ Don't Size Up for Growing Room

This is the #1 skate mistake new hockey parents make. Skates that are too big cause the ankle to fold over, make it impossible to skate properly, and lead to pain and blisters. Buy skates that fit now — not skates they'll grow into.

The Proper Fit Test

  • 👆
    Toe test with bent knee. Have your player stand with knees bent in a hockey stance. The toes should lightly brush the front of the skate — not jammed, not with a big gap.
  • 🦶
    Heel should be locked in. No heel lift when the player is standing. If the heel lifts when they walk, the skate is too big or too wide.
  • 👟
    Width matters too. Many brands offer different widths (D = standard, EE = wide). A player with a wide foot in a narrow skate will always be uncomfortable regardless of length.
  • ⏱️
    Try them on at the end of the day. Feet swell slightly throughout the day — fitting skates in the morning can lead to tight skates by game time.
💡 Get Them Baked

Heat molding (baking) new skates at a pro shop custom-forms the boot to the foot's shape. It dramatically reduces break-in time and prevents blisters. Most shops charge $10–$20 and it's absolutely worth it for any skate over $100.

When to Replace Skates

Youth players can outgrow skates in as little as one season. Signs it's time for new skates: toes are crushed against the front, the boot is collapsing at the ankle, or blades are too worn to sharpen effectively. Don't wait until the start of the season — pro shops get backed up and baking appointments fill fast.

02

Stick Length & Fit

A stick that's the wrong length makes everything harder — skating, stickhandling, shooting. The right length lets a player skate upright and handle the puck comfortably without hunching over or reaching up.

The Standard Length Rule

Without skates on: The stick should reach between the chin and the nose when stood vertically on the floor next to the player.

With skates on: Skates add roughly 1–1.5 inches of height. With skates on, the stick should reach between the chin and the collarbone.

💡 Position Preference

Defensemen often prefer a slightly longer stick for better reach and poke-checking. Forwards, especially playmakers, often go slightly shorter for better puck control. These are tendencies, not rules — let your player experiment.

03

Stick Flex

Flex is how much resistance the stick has when you load it during a shot. Too stiff and the player can't load the stick — shots feel weak. Too whippy and they lose control and accuracy. Getting flex right makes a real difference in shot power and feel.

The general rule: flex should be roughly half the player's body weight in pounds. A 100-lb player would use a 50-flex stick. This is a starting point — some players go slightly stiffer or whippier based on feel.

⚠️ Cutting a Stick Changes the Flex

Every inch you cut off the top of a stick increases the effective flex by about 3–5 points. If you buy a 50-flex stick and cut 4 inches off, it plays closer to a 65-flex. Factor this in when buying — especially for growing players who need sticks cut down.

04

Stick Lie

Lie is the angle between the blade and the shaft. It determines where the blade sits flat on the ice when a player is in their natural skating stance. Most youth sticks come in a lie of 5, 5.5, or 6 — and most youth players do well with a standard lie 5 or 5.5.

💡 Quick Lie Check

Have your player stand in their normal skating stance on a flat surface. If the toe of the blade is lifting off the floor, the lie is too high. If the heel is lifting, it's too low. The whole blade should rest flat when they're in their natural position.

05

Blade Curve

The curve of the blade affects how a player handles the puck, shoots, and receives passes. For youth players, this matters less than fit and flex — but it's worth understanding as your player develops.

  • 🏒
    Mid-curve (most common for youth). Versatile — good for stickhandling and shooting. Great starting point for new players and most youth levels. The Bauer P92 and CCM P29 pattern are popular mid-curves.
  • 🎯
    Heel curve. Better for slapshots and harder one-timers. Less common in youth but some defensemen prefer it for blue-line shots.
  • 🤹
    Toe curve. Better for dekes and toe drags. More popular with skilled forwards who handle the puck a lot in tight spaces.
  • ↔️
    Curve depth (open vs. closed). A more open face helps lift the puck. A closed face gives more control and accuracy on flat passes. Most youth sticks fall somewhere in the middle.
✅ For New Players

Don't overthink curve for beginners. A standard mid-curve in the right size and flex will serve any new player well. Save the blade obsession for when they're skating well and asking their own questions.

06

Sizing as Your Player Grows

Youth players can go through multiple stick and skate sizes in a single season. Here's how to manage it without constantly spending a fortune.

  • 📅
    Check fit at the start of every season. Even if gear "still fits," growth spurts can happen fast. A skate that fit in March may be too small by September.
  • 💰
    Buy used at younger ages. Kids under 10 outgrow gear so fast that buying new every season is expensive. Facebook Marketplace, Play It Again Sports, and team swap meets are great resources.
  • 🏒
    Sticks are consumables. At higher levels sticks break frequently. Buying mid-range sticks in bulk (or during sales) is smarter than one expensive stick that snaps in a tournament.
  • ⛸️
    Skates are worth investing in. Unlike sticks, quality skates make a real performance difference. As your player gets older and more serious, a better-fitting skate with a stiffer boot pays off in skating improvement.
  • 📏
    Measure feet twice a year. Feet can grow a full size between September and March. Keep a note of your player's current foot length in cm so you can shop online accurately.
💡 The Two-Finger Rule for Skates

A quick in-store check: slide the player's foot forward until the toes touch the front of the unlaced skate. You should be able to fit two fingers behind the heel. One finger = probably right size. No fingers = too small. Three fingers = too big.
