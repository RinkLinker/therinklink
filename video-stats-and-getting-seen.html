Video, Stats, and Getting Seen

How to build a highlight video coaches will actually watch, what to track, and how to put it together into something that gets a response.

🏆 AAA & Elite · 9 min read · Built by hockey families
01

Is Sending Video Actually Helpful?

Yes, genuinely. As covered in our guide on when players start getting noticed, coaches can't legally initiate contact with a recruit before a certain point, but video is one of the tools that gets a player onto a coach's radar well before that date, and stays useful long after it too.

Why It Works

Video lets a coach evaluate hundreds of players without traveling to see each one in person, and it's something staff can revisit, share among assistants, and compare across a whole recruiting class. According to the National Field Hockey Coaches Association's own survey of college coaches, video consistently ranked among the top things coaches said they wanted from recruits, right alongside being contacted directly.

Source: NFHCA, Video Advice from College Coaches

02

What Actually Belongs in the Video

Recruiting services that specialize in this, including NCSA and SportsRecruits, converge on a similar formula: keep it to three to six minutes, roughly 20 to 30 clips, and lead with your single best play. Coaches typically decide whether to keep watching within the first 30 seconds, so the strongest clip goes first, not buried in the middle.

Rule of Thumb Why
3–6 minutes, 20–30 clips Long enough to show range, short enough that coaches actually finish it
Best clip first Coaches decide to keep watching in the first 30 seconds
Game footage over drills Shows decision-making under real pressure, not just isolated skill
Skip the music, add a closing slide Many coaches watch on mute; name, position, grad year, and coach contact matter more

Game footage matters more than skills or drill footage for skaters specifically, since it shows a player reading the ice and making decisions under real pressure, something a controlled drill can't demonstrate. Goalies are the one exception, coaches expect and want to see some clean save-selection and technique footage alongside game clips. Shoot at a wide enough angle to show how a play develops, not just a tight shot on the puck.

Worth Knowing

One recruiting video company that's produced thousands of these, SportsRecruits, surveyed the college coaches they work with directly and found coaches spend an average of about three minutes and fifteen seconds watching a single video. That's a useful gut-check: if your video runs eight minutes, most of it isn't getting watched.

03

What Information Players Should Be Tracking

Beyond video, the other half of getting noticed is having your actual information organized and ready to share the moment a coach asks, rather than scrambling to compile it after the fact.

Core Stats
  • + Goals, assists, points by season
  • + Team, league, and level played at each season
  • + Awards, all-star selections, championships
  • + Physical testing data if available (speed, conditioning)
Profile Info
  • ✕ Current academic info, GPA, grad year
  • ✕ Height, weight, position, shot side
  • ✕ Current coach's name and contact info
  • ✕ Camps, showcases, and tournaments attended

A verified profile on a platform like Elite Prospects has effectively become a player's hockey resume, according to Elite Prospects' own guidance for players. When a coach hears a name mentioned after a tournament and searches it that evening, that profile is often the first thing that comes up, so keeping it accurate and current matters more than most players realize.

What Coaches Are Actually Looking For, Beyond the Stat Line

Elite Prospects notes that scouts track detail well beyond goals and assists, things like decision-making under pressure, how a player responds after a mistake, and general coachability. College coaches in particular are making four-year commitments to a person, not just a stat line, and they'll often talk to a player's coaches or teammates to get a read on character alongside skill. That's not something a highlight video can show, but it's worth knowing it's part of the evaluation.

Source: Elite Prospects, How to Use EP to Get Noticed by Hockey Scouts

04

Putting It All Together

Once the video and the stats exist, they need to actually reach a coach. Most recruiting platforms let a player build a shareable profile page, and it's worth sending video and stats directly to specific programs a player is genuinely interested in rather than a mass blast to every school in a conference.

College Hockey Inc., the NCAA's own recruiting resource, points out something worth remembering here: college hockey has no draft, so a player genuinely has a say in where they end up, which makes a direct, targeted approach to programs of real interest more valuable than a scattershot one.

Update the video and stats through the season, not just once. Recruiting services consistently advise reordering the strongest clips to the front as new footage comes in, and resending an updated profile after a strong showcase or tournament performance, referencing that specific event, tends to land better than a generic follow-up.

05

A Template Worth Copying

Recruiting services that specialize in coach outreach, including NCSA and hockey-specific recruiting site Ivy Hockey, converge on the same core structure for a first email: introduction, then athletic and academic snapshot, then a clear next step. Coaches scan hundreds of these, so fact-first and specific beats a long personal story every time.

Copy, Fill In, and Send

SUBJECT: [Your Full Name] | [Grad Year] | [Position] | [Key Stat or Event] Dear Coach [Last Name], My name is [your name], and I'm a [grade level] at [high school/club team name], where I play [position]. I graduate in [year]. This past season I recorded [# goals, # assists, # points] playing [level/league], and [one more standout stat, award, or team result]. I'm very interested in [program name] because [one specific, real reason, playing style, coaching staff, academics, something you've actually researched, not generic]. Here's a link to my highlight video: [link]. My current coach, [name], can be reached at [email/phone] if you'd like more insight into my game. I'd welcome the chance to talk further or answer any questions. Thank you for your time and consideration. [Your name] [Phone number] [Email address]

Do
  • + Send to the head coach and all assistants; the recruiting contact varies by school
  • + Confirm the highlight video link actually works before sending
  • + Personalize the "why this program" line for every school
  • + Send a real update roughly every 3 months, new stats, video, or a season result
Don't
  • ✕ Copy-paste the same "why this program" line to every school
  • ✕ Panic if there's no reply for a couple of weeks; coaches are busy
  • ✕ Send a nearly identical follow-up with nothing new to report
  • ✕ Get the coach's name, title, or program details wrong

One important note: if the recruit is a younger player, NCAA rules restrict when a coach is legally allowed to respond, as covered in our guide on when players get noticed. A player is generally allowed to send an email at any age, taking that initiative early tends to be viewed as a sign of maturity, even if the coach can't respond yet.

Video and stats don't replace development or ice time, but they make sure the development that's already happening actually gets seen. Keep both current, keep the video short, and send it to the programs that are actually a real fit.
